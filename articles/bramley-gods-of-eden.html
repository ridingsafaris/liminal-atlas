🔎 Author background — the Tore Dahlin problem

“William Bramley” is a pseudonym for Tore Bjørn Dahlin, a California attorney and former Scientology staff member (Clear, Sea Org, CCLA). The Church of Scientology formally banned this book in 1995 via Executive Directive SO ED 4577 INT. Ex-Scientologist researcher Jim Keith documented that the book systematically delivers Hubbard’s cosmology to a UFO audience — the Third Party Law, soul/body dualism, graduated liberation, anti-psychiatry — without disclosing its Scientology origin. Dahlin was disbarred in California in 2012 for misappropriating client funds.

This context does not invalidate Bramley’s historical source material — the Masonic histories, Rosicrucian records, and archival documents he cites are often real. It does mean the interpretive framework deserves scrutiny.

Ch. 1 The Search Begins

The Search Begins

📋 Summary

Bramley opens with his origin story: a childhood horror at documentary footage of Nazi concentration camps that left him asking why human beings wage war. He began researching in 1979, expecting to find economic profiteering — banking elites, arms merchants — as the hidden engine of organised conflict. The “third party” concept (a hidden actor who profits from others’ fighting, whether they are parties to the conflict or not) is introduced as his analytical frame. After years of research, he found something far stranger than he expected. The book’s entire edifice rests on this stated reluctance — his credibility depends on having arrived at his conclusions unwillingly.

“I will admit that when I began my research I had a bias about what I was expecting to find: a human profit motive as the common thread which links various third-party influences in mankind’s violent history. What I found instead was the UFO. Nothing could have been more unwelcome.”
— Bramley, Ch. 1

The “Third Party Law” Bramley presents here as his own original insight is a near-verbatim reproduction of L. Ron Hubbard’s 1968 Scientology policy letter “The Third Party Law,” which Bramley — as a former Scientology staff member — would have been intimately familiar with. The concept is presented without attribution throughout the book.

Ch. 2 Orientation

Orientation

📋 Summary

A scene-setting orientation: Earth as small, isolated, and inhabited by a creature that is more than animal — a spiritual being. Bramley argues the soul/body distinction is ancient wisdom now lost or obscured. This chapter introduces his metaphysical baseline: individual spiritual beings (souls) incarnate in bodies and survive death. Without this premise, his later argument that the Custodians exploit spiritual ignorance makes no sense. The chapter also introduces the idea that ancient spiritual knowledge was once clear and has since been systematically corrupted — a theme that will drive the entire book.

“When dealing with a human being, are we only confronting an animal? As it turns out, we are not. It appears that we are faced with something much more important: a spiritual being.”
— Bramley, Ch. 2

The soul/body framework Bramley establishes here — individual spiritual beings trapped in matter, capable of liberation through knowledge — is foundational Scientology cosmology. Hubbard’s concept of the “thetan” (an individual immortal spiritual being) is being delivered here in secular anthropological language.

Ch. 3 UFOs: Truth or Fiction?

UFOs: Truth or Fiction?

📋 Summary

Bramley’s long UFO evidence chapter: a survey of ancient sightings (Julius Obsequens, 216 BC; Cicero; Charlemagne’s edicts against aerial disturbances), through 20th-century cases. He makes the now-familiar argument that 3–8.5% of UFO reports remain genuinely unexplained. He covers Project Blue Book, the Robertson Panel, the Condon Report, and the question of institutional suppression. His key historical framing: UFOs have been present throughout recorded history and are inseparable from major events — wars, plagues, religious movements.

“Roughly 90–95% of all reported UFOs prove to be man-made aircraft or unrecognized natural phenomena. The remaining 3–8.5% of all UFO sightings are those which appear to be aircraft of nonhuman origin.”
— Bramley, Ch. 3
⚠ Disputed / disproven

The 3–8.5% figure is Bramley’s extrapolation from older data and is not cited to any specific study. Modern AARO and Pentagon data suggest similar unresolved percentages but with very different case profiles. The Condon Report was deeply flawed (as J. Allen Hynek and James McDonald documented), but it was not simply a straightforward cover-up.

Ch. 4 The Gods of Eden

The Gods of Eden

📋 Summary

The book’s central claim, stated plainly: humanity is a slave race engineered by an extraterrestrial society. Bramley coins the term “Custodians” for this society to avoid the loaded connotations of “gods” or “ancient astronauts.” He draws on the Atrahasis Epic (“When the gods like men bore the work and suffered the toil”) and Zecharia Sitchin’s readings of Sumerian tablets to argue the Anunnaki created Homo sapiens as a labour force. The Custodians are described as physically humanlike, racially diverse, behaviourally spanning the full spectrum from saints to despots — but with the brutal, despotic element historically dominant. The Eden narrative in this reading: humans were created as unknowing slaves, the “nakedness” signifying unawareness of their enslaved condition.

“The idea that human beings are a slave race owned by an extraterrestrial society is not a new one. It was expressed thousands of years ago in mankind’s earliest recorded civilizations.”
— Bramley, Ch. 4
⚠ Disputed / disproven

Bramley’s Custodian thesis relies almost entirely on Zecharia Sitchin’s translations of Sumerian cuneiform, which mainstream Assyriology has rejected. Michael S. Heiser’s detailed philological analysis (The Myth of a Twelfth Planet) demonstrates that Sitchin misread key terms, including Anunnaki (“those of royal blood” or “princely offspring,” not “those who from heaven to Earth came”). The Atrahasis Epic’s narrative of humans created to relieve divine labour is real — the interpretive leap to literal extraterrestrial bioengineering is Sitchin’s and Bramley’s, not the text’s.

✓ Verifiable / confirmed

The Atrahasis Epic is a genuine cuneiform text predating the Biblical flood narrative. The creation of humans from divine blood and clay to perform labour the gods found burdensome is a real element of Mesopotamian mythology. The interpretive framework is disputed; the source document is authentic.

Ch. 5 Brotherhood of the Snake

Brotherhood of the Snake

📋 Summary

The chapter that establishes the book’s institutional engine. The Brotherhood of the Snake was founded by Ea/Enki — the rebellious Custodial figure identified with the Biblical serpent in Eden — as a secret organisation dedicated to genuine spiritual liberation. Ea’s motive: he regretted creating a slave race and tried to give humanity real spiritual knowledge (“the fruit of the tree of knowledge”). The Brotherhood was quickly captured by rival Custodial factions, Ea was vilified as Satan, and the organisation was turned from a liberation movement into a tool of spiritual repression. Bramley argues this corrupted Brotherhood is the common institutional ancestor of every major secret society in human history, from Egyptian mystery schools through the Templars, Rosicrucians, and Freemasons. The snake symbol (Caduceus, AMA logo) is its surviving trace.

“The Brotherhood of the Snake opposed the enslavement of spiritual beings and, according to Egyptian writings, it sought to liberate the human race from Custodial bondage.”
— Bramley, Ch. 5
“Ea’s title was changed from ‘Prince of Earth’ to ‘Prince of Darkness.’ He was labelled: Satan, the Devil, Evil Incarnate, Monarch of Hell, Lord of Vermin, Prince of Liars.”
— Bramley, Ch. 5

This chapter’s claim — that a corrupted secret Brotherhood is the common ancestor of Freemasonry, Rosicrucianism, and all major esoteric traditions — is the book’s most consequential structural argument. Everything in the historical chapters that follows is a case study in how this Brotherhood operates. The Ea-as-Lucifer identification is a speculative synthesis across mythological traditions that professional scholars of comparative religion do not support.

Ch. 6 The Pyramid Builders

The Pyramid Builders

📋 Summary

Bramley analyses the Great Pyramid’s extraordinary precision — cardinal orientation accurate to one-twelfth of a degree, positioned at the centre of Earth’s landmasses, with a 30-60-90 degree grid function ideal for aerial navigation — as evidence that the Brotherhood built it under Custodial direction, primarily as an aerial navigation marker. The original white limestone casing would have made it visible from great distances. The Egyptian mystery schools are traced to Brotherhood guilds: the papyri discovered by Flinders Petrie in 1888–89 describe a guild meeting around 2000 BC with “Warden” and “Master” titles identical to modern Freemasonry. The Egyptian Book of the Dead contained teachings from the Brotherhood’s Egyptian Mystery Schools — a watered-down version of the original spiritual knowledge.

“The Great Pyramid is an excellent marker for aerial navigation. The four sides precisely face the four compass points. The Great Pyramid is situated less than five miles south of the northern thirtieth parallel.”
— Bramley, Ch. 6
“One set [of papyri] described a guild that held secret meetings around the year 2000 B.C. The guild met to discuss working hours, wages, and rules for daily labour… The organisational duties described in the papyri are very similar to those of ‘Warden’ and ‘Master’ in a modern branch of the Brotherhood which evolved from those guilds: Freemasonry.”
— Bramley, Ch. 6
✓ Verifiable / confirmed

The Petrie excavations at Kahun (1888–89) did discover administrative papyri describing organised labour guilds with hierarchical roles. The interpretation of these as direct Masonic ancestors is Bramley’s. The Great Pyramid’s precision measurements are accurate.

⚠ Disputed / disproven

No evidence connects the Pyramid’s construction to aerial navigation for Custodial craft. The Masonic tradition’s own historians trace speculative Freemasonry to 17th-century Scotland (Stevenson) or at earliest to medieval stonemasons’ guilds — not to Egyptian papyri from 2000 BC.

Ch. 7 Jehovah

Jehovah

📋 Summary

Bramley reads the Old Testament Jehovah through the UFO lens: a being who descended in fire and smoke, issued commandments from a machine that produced “thunders and lightnings,” destroyed cities with aerial weapons, led the Hebrew tribes in a “pillar of cloud by day, pillar of fire by night,” and was jealous, violent, and tribe-specific. Jehovah was not the Supreme Being of the cosmos but a Custodial individual — a specific extraterrestrial “god” with his own political agenda for the Hebrew nation. The Ark of the Covenant is interpreted as a dangerous electrical/radio device — capable of killing those who touched it improperly. Moses received the tablets and the Temple blueprint from a Custodial presence, not from a universal God.

“Jehovah travelled through the sky in what appears to have been a noisy, smoking aircraft… And Mount Sinai was altogether covered with smoke, because the Lord descended upon it in fire: and the smoke from the fire billowed upwards like the smoke of a furnace, and the whole mountain quaked greatly.”
— Bramley, Ch. 7
⚠ Disputed / disproven

The “Jehovah as extraterrestrial” reading of theophany language — pioneered by von Däniken before Bramley — treats ancient Near Eastern divine-presence imagery as engineering description. Biblical scholars note that “fire and smoke” theophany is standard Semitic literary convention for divine manifestation, not a technical account. The Ark’s lethal electrical properties have been investigated but no physical mechanism has been identified.

Ch. 8 Melchizedek's Apron

Melchizedek's Apron

📋 Summary

The chapter most directly concerned with Freemasonry’s origins. Solomon built his famous Temple using Egyptian Brotherhood mason guilds; the guilds used Brotherhood ranks (Warden, Master), practiced a mystical tradition, and were organised as the Brotherhood’s construction arm. Melchizedek — the mysterious priest-king of Salem who appears briefly in Genesis to give Abraham bread and wine — is identified as a Brotherhood adept. His apron (a detail from rabbinic commentary) is Bramley’s segue to the Masonic apron, a central Craft symbol still worn today. The chapter traces the Brotherhood’s mystery school tradition through Solomon’s Temple, the Egyptian Mystery Schools (“House of Life” temples), and into the earliest operative mason guilds. Hiram Abiff — the legendary architect of Solomon’s Temple murdered for refusing to reveal Masonic secrets, whose death and symbolic resurrection is the centrepiece of the Third Degree initiation — appears here as the Brotherhood’s martyred representative.

“Solomon brought in special guilds of masons to design his buildings and to oversee their construction. Those special guilds were already important institutions in Egypt, and because the guilds were Brotherhood organisations, they used many Brotherhood ranks and titles. The organisational duties described in the papyri are very similar to those of ‘Warden’ and ‘Master’ in a modern branch of the Brotherhood which evolved from those guilds: Freemasonry.”
— Bramley, Ch. 8

This chapter delivers the book’s most explicit Masonic thesis. Bramley is arguing that Freemasonry is not a 17th-century Scottish or 18th-century English invention but an unbroken institutional continuity from the Egyptian Brotherhood through the Jerusalem mason guilds of Solomon’s time. The Masonic lodge’s symbolic geography (Temple of Solomon, Warden positions, Master Mason degree with its Hiram Abiff death-and-resurrection narrative) is treated as authentic institutional memory rather than 18th-century mythmaking.

✓ Verifiable / confirmed

The Masonic lodge’s central ritual does use Temple of Solomon symbolism and the Hiram Abiff narrative. The Masonic apron is a real and important symbol. These are documented facts of Masonic practice.

⚠ Disputed / disproven

Mainstream historians of Freemasonry (David Stevenson, J.M. Roberts) place the emergence of speculative Masonry in 17th-century Scotland. The Egyptian Brotherhood-Solomon’s Temple-Freemasonry continuity is Masonic legend, promoted by sources like H. Spencer Lewis’s AMORC histories, which Bramley cites extensively. There is no documentary evidence connecting ancient Egyptian mason guilds to any precursor of Freemasonry.

Ch. 9 Gods and Aryans

Gods and Aryans

📋 Summary

Chapter 9 (“Gods and Aryans”) argues that the Aryan migrations were directed by Brotherhood missionaries who deliberately introduced a corrupted spiritual system into India — the varna (caste) system as a Custodial tool of social stratification disguised as spiritual order. Bramley is careful here: the soul-matter dualism of Samkhya (“souls are infinite in number and consist of pure intelligence, each soul independent and immortal”) is presented approvingly as authentic ancient wisdom.

“Souls are infinite in number and consist of pure intelligence. Each soul is independent, indivisible, unconditioned, incapable of change, immortal. It appears, however, to be bound in matter.”
— Samkhya system, quoted by Bramley Ch. 9

The Samkhya definition Bramley quotes approvingly here — an infinite number of individual immortal souls bound in matter — is structurally identical to Scientology’s account of the “thetan.” This passage is one of those Jim Keith identified as Bramley implicitly citing Hubbard’s cosmology under the cover of ancient Indian philosophy.

Ch. 11 Doom Prophets

Doom Prophets

📋 Summary

Bramley examines the phoenix symbol as a Brotherhood/Custodial control mechanism. The phoenix — a bird that burns itself and rises from the ashes — is an Egyptian Brotherhood emblem that became the mythological basis for apocalyptic religion. End-of-the-world doctrines, Bramley argues, were disseminated by Brotherhood missionaries as a tool of social control: “obey or die” theology. The Zoroastrian Final Battle, the Jewish Day of Judgment, the Christian Armageddon, and the Islamic apocalypse all follow the same template — all imposed on humanity by the same Brotherhood network under Custodial direction. The phoenix symbol itself survived into modern Freemasonry and was used on early American state seals (it was the original suggestion for the US Seal before the eagle replaced it).

“End of the World doctrines also make people afraid to explore competing religious systems. Judgment Day teachings ultimately amount to extortion: obey or die.”
— Bramley, Ch. 11

The phoenix’s documented presence in early American symbolism — Benjamin Franklin’s original proposal for the Great Seal was a phoenix — and in Masonic iconography is a verifiable historical fact. Bramley uses this to argue institutional continuity between the Egyptian Brotherhood and American founding-era Masonry.

✓ Verifiable / confirmed

The phoenix was indeed proposed for the US Great Seal by Benjamin Franklin. The phoenix is a documented symbol in Masonic ritual. Its Egyptian origins are real.

Ch. 12 The Jesus Ministry

The Jesus Ministry

📋 Summary

Jesus is read as a genuine Brotherhood reformer — someone attempting to restore direct spiritual liberation outside the corrupted priestly hierarchy. His teachings on the soul’s independence and spiritual freedom are authentic, says Bramley. But the early Christian church was captured: the councils of Nicaea (325 AD) and Constantinople (553 AD) deleted reincarnation from scripture, the Lateran Councils added the Holy Trinity (never taught by Jesus), and whole books were suppressed. The Apocrypha (“hidden writings”) preserved what the official canon destroyed. Bramley reads the Immaculate Conception narrative through the Apocrypha: Mary was raised in a Brotherhood temple by “priests and angels” who arranged her marriage and whose “angel Gabriel” impregnated her — suggesting Custodial involvement in Jesus’s conception. Chapter 13 covers the Apocalypse of John as a genuine UFO contact experience, with Revelation’s strange “creatures” identified as Custodial beings giving scripted prophecy. Chapter 14 examines the Justinian Plagues (541–549 AD) — read, like the Black Death, as a Custodially-timed outbreak accompanied by UFO sightings. Chapter 15 applies the same template to Mohammed: a genuine spiritual reformer whose revelations came through a Custodial “angel,” and whose message was then captured into a hierarchical militant religion. Chapter 16 (“Messiahs and Means”) proposes a theory of authentic spiritual liberation: a real “messiah” would be someone who successfully develops a functional science of the spirit — the title would be earned, not prophetically assigned.

“Many of the changes and deletions to the New Testament were made by special church councils. The editing process began as early as 325 A.D. during the First Council of Nicea… The Second Synod of Constantinople in 553 A.D. deleted from the Bible Jesus’s references to ‘reincarnation.’”
— Bramley, Ch. 12
⚠ Disputed / disproven

Jesus’s alleged teachings on reincarnation are not documented in surviving pre-Nicene texts. The Council of Nicaea (325 AD) primarily addressed Arianism (the nature of Christ), not reincarnation. There is no historical record of a council specifically voting to remove reincarnation from the Bible. Bramley’s claim here is widely repeated in New Age literature but lacks documentary support.

Ch. 17 Flying Gods Over America

Flying Gods Over America

📋 Summary

Bramley surveys the pre-Columbian American civilisations — Olmec, Maya, Aztec, Inca — through the same Brotherhood lens. The similarities between Old and New World civilisations (pyramids, mummification, solar worship, priest hierarchies, sacrificial religion) are too strong for independent invention, he argues. The common source is the Brotherhood network operating globally under Custodial direction. Quetzalcoatl — the feathered serpent deity of Mesoamerica — is identified as a Brotherhood representative (the serpent symbol again). The conquest of the Americas by Spanish Catholics is read as one Brotherhood faction (the Inquisition) destroying another (the indigenous Brotherhood organisations). The human sacrifice practiced by the Aztecs is attributed to Custodial corruption of the original Brotherhood’s benign spiritual programme.

“The coincidences are far too strong for the American civilisations to have arisen independently of the Old World societies. The striking similarities indicate that the American civilisations were part of a global society.”
— Bramley, Ch. 17
⚠ Disputed / disproven

The diffusionist hypothesis — that American civilisations derived their pyramid-building from Egyptian or Mesopotamian contact — is rejected by mainstream archaeology. Pyramids arose independently in multiple cultures as structurally obvious solutions for monumental construction. Similarities in solar worship and priest hierarchies reflect common human responses to astronomical observation, not institutional contact.

Ch. 18 The Black Death

The Black Death

📋 Summary

The book’s most famous chapter and the one cited by Jason Jorjani. Bramley argues the Black Death (1347–51, 25–35 million dead) was a Custodially-engineered bioweapon deployment. His evidence chain: (1) Many outbreaks occurred in warm weather in uncrowded regions without prior rodent infestation — anomalies the standard flea vector model does not explain. (2) Contemporary accounts describe “foul-smelling mists” appearing after “bright lights in the sky” immediately before outbreaks. (3) Hooded figures with spray-canister devices were reported walking through towns and spraying doorways before plague broke out — and this imagery became the basis for the Grim Reaper iconography (hooded, scythe as stylised spray device). (4) The US and USSR maintain stockpiles of bubonic plague in aerosol dispersal bioweapons, proving the mechanism is feasible. The chapter also cites Bishop Agobard of Lyon’s 9th-century account of the Magonia aerial ships and the Charlemagne-era “tyrants of the air” as historical precedent for Custodial aerial operations over Europe.

“The plague broke out only after these people would land in the fields and go around with these hoods on and these spray cans… The shrouded figures, and the tools from which stuff would spray, became the basis for the scythe and the Grim Reaper image.”
— Bramley, Ch. 18
“At the time the Black Death breaks out, there are many UFO sightings. There are sightings of objects that land in fields. It was so prominent that hysteria broke out about it in the French court and Charlemagne was considering declaring war on the tyrants of the air.”
— Bramley, Ch. 18
⚠ Disputed / disproven

The 2010 Haensch et al. study confirmed Yersinia pestis DNA in Black Death burial pits, establishing the biological agent. The 2018 Dean et al. study argued human body lice (not rats) were the primary transmission vector — which does explain some warm-weather, low-rodent anomalies Bramley highlights, but through ectoparasite transmission rather than bioweapon spraying.

✓ Verifiable / confirmed

Bishop Agobard of Lyon’s 9th-century document about “aerial ships” and “tyrants of the air” is a genuine historical text reproduced and discussed seriously by Jacques Vallée in Passport to Magonia (1969). The Grim Reaper’s connection to plague iconography is documented. The anomalous plague outbreaks Bramley cites have been noted by epidemiological historians.

◆ Open questions
  • What are Bramley’s primary sources for the specific “hooded figures with spray canisters” accounts? Do they appear in the original medieval chronicles he cites, or are they synthesised from multiple sources?
  • If the body-lice transmission model explains warm-weather outbreaks without rodents, does Bramley’s argument for aerial dispersal collapse entirely?

Ch. 19 Luther and the Rose

Luther and the Rose

📋 Summary

This chapter marks a major shift in the book: the history of the Brotherhood becomes explicitly the history of Freemasonry and Rosicrucianism. Bramley states directly that the “Illuminati” — the Brotherhood’s advanced inner circle — became concentrated in the German principalities, particularly Hesse. The Rosicrucian Order is introduced as “one of the Illuminati’s most important branches in Germany,” established with its first lodge at Worms in Hesse in 1100 AD (after Charlemagne introduced it in the 9th century). The 108-year cycles of “activity” and “inactivity” — explaining why the Rosicrucians seemed to appear and disappear from history — are described in detail: each branch alternated between public recruitment phases and complete underground concealment, explaining the confusion in later histories about when Rosicrucianism “started.” Martin Luther is positioned as a genuine reformer whose efforts were partially co-opted by the Brotherhood — the Reformation became the vehicle for a new wave of Brotherhood activity dressed as religious renewal.

“One of the Illuminati’s most important branches in Germany was the mystical Rosicrucian organisation. Germany’s first official Rosicrucian Lodge was established in the city of Worms in the year 1100 A.D. Advancement through the Rosicrucian degrees often resulted in admittance to the Illuminati.”
— Bramley, Ch. 19
“Each Rosicrucian branch was required to compute successive 108-year periods — one of ‘outward’ activity during which the branch’s existence would be made widely known, the next of concealed, silent activity. As branches switched between phases, it seemed to observers that Rosicrucian Orders were appearing and disappearing in history.”
— Bramley, Ch. 19

This is the chapter where Bramley’s Rosicrucian material becomes most specific and institutionally detailed. The 108-year cycle claim comes from H. Spencer Lewis’s AMORC histories — a source with obvious institutional interest in promoting a long pedigree for Rosicrucianism. Compare with Frances Yates (The Rosicrucian Enlightenment, 1972), who dates the German Rosicrucian manifestos to 1614–16 as their historical emergence.

Ch. 20 A New Aristocracy

A New Aristocracy

📋 Summary

Four chapters tracing Brotherhood machinations through the Eighty Years War, the Dutch Republic, the English banking system, and the Glorious Revolution of 1688. The House of Orange-Nassau (“a treacherous German clique” of royal families master at marrying into foreign dynasties as a stepping stone to power) is identified as a key Brotherhood vehicle. The Dutch parliamentary system — a weakened monarchy paired with a strong central bank — is presented as the Brotherhood’s preferred political model, replicated wherever the network seized power. Ch. 21 (“Funny Money”) is a detailed critique of fractional reserve banking: how paper money began as promissory notes and was transformed into debt slavery by allowing banks to issue far more notes than they held in gold, creating perpetual indebtedness. Ch. 22 (“Marching Saints”) covers Calvin’s predestination theology as Brotherhood spiritual control — a philosophy designed to make people accept their social position as God’s will. Ch. 23 (“William and Mary Have a War”): William III of Orange, reported Freemason, plants the Bank of England (1694) under a Scottish adventurer named William Paterson — establishing the inflatable currency model that would finance centuries of Brotherhood-directed warfare. The Orange Order is noted as formed in 1688 patterned on Freemasonry.

“Upon his assumption of the British throne, William III quickly undertook to erect the same institutions in England as those established by his dynasty in Holland: a strong parliament with a weakened monarchy and a central bank operating on an inflatable paper currency.”
— Bramley, Ch. 23
“Behind this intrigue we see the shadow of the Brotherhood. William III is reported to have been a Freemason. In 1688, a militant secret society was formed to support William III — the Order of Orange — patterned itself after Freemasonry.”

GLOSSARY8 key terms

Key Terms

Custodians
Bramley’s term for the extraterrestrial society he believes has owned Earth since prehistory, avoiding the loaded connotations of “gods” or “ancient astronauts.” Physically humanlike, behaviourally diverse, with the despotic faction historically dominant.
Brotherhood of the Snake
The secret organisation founded by Ea/Enki as a spiritual liberation movement. Captured by rival Custodial factions and turned into a tool of repression. All subsequent secret societies — Egyptian mystery schools, Knights Templar, Rosicrucians, Freemasons — are its corrupted branches.
Third Party Law
Bramley’s stated original analytical principle: wars and conflicts are maintained by a hidden third party profiting from the violence. This is a near-verbatim reproduction of L. Ron Hubbard’s 1968 Scientology policy letter of the same name.
Operative vs. Speculative Freemasonry
Operative Freemasonry: actual stonemasons organised in trade guilds. Speculative Freemasonry: the fraternal-mystical system that replaced it, formalised at the 1717 Grand Lodge founding. Bramley traces speculative Freemasonry’s symbolic vocabulary directly to the Egyptian Brotherhood guilds.
Strict Observance
An 18th-century Masonic system requiring absolute obedience to an “Unknown Superior” said to reside in Scotland. Associated with Jacobite (pro-Stuart) politics and the creation of Templar-themed higher degrees. The German princes of Hesse were its leading patrons.
Illuminati (Bramley)
Bramley uses “Illuminati” to mean the Brotherhood’s genuine inner circle — distinct from Weishaupt’s 1776 Bavarian Illuminati, which Bramley calls a “bogus Illuminati” created within a Strict Observance lodge. The genuine Illuminati concentrated in the German principalities, particularly Hesse.
Custodial Religion
Religions constructed by Custodial agents or the corrupted Brotherhood to keep humans spiritually ignorant and obedient to priestly intermediaries. Characterised by monotheism, apocalyptic threat, graduated initiation, and suppression of direct spiritual access. Distinguished from the “maverick religions” (Buddhism, early Christianity, Islam) which were genuine reform movements later captured.
