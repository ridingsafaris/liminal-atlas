Emanuel Swedenborg —
The Earths in the Universe

Author: Emanuel Swedenborg (1688–1772) Full title: De Telluribus in Mundo Nostro Solari Published: 1758 113 pages · §1–178 Related: Arcana Coelestia close reading

In 1763, Immanuel Kant read this book and found himself unable to simply dismiss it. In 1766 he published an ambivalent “debunking” that engaged Swedenborg far more seriously than it pretended to. In 1781 he published the Critique of Pure Reason — which systematically places claims like Swedenborg’s permanently beyond rational investigation. Jason Jorjani argues this sequence is not coincidence. This close reading covers Swedenborg’s first-person accounts of beings from Mercury (interstellar telepaths who read minds like books), Mars (celestial communicators whose speech bypasses the ear and enters through the Eustachian tube), Jupiter (a Golden Age civilisation that turns away in horror from descriptions of war), and five earths orbiting other suns — reached by shifting inner states rather than crossing physical distance.

Jorjani’s ClaimWhy This Book Made Kant Panic

Why This Book Made Kant Panic

📋 Summary

In his American Alchemy episode with Jesse Michels, Jason Jorjani makes a specific argument about Immanuel Kant and Emanuel Swedenborg. Jorjani claims that Kant, in his youth, carefully read the entirety of Swedenborg’s Arcana Coelestia — including these accounts of extraterrestrial intelligences living on other planets, conducting what Swedenborg reports as telepathic communication, navigating the spirit world, and visiting Swedenborg directly. Kant’s response, says Jorjani, was not simple dismissal: his early (suppressed) writings reportedly acknowledge that the Swedenborgian evidence was too destabilising — politically and socially — to be released without a philosophical containment strategy. The Critique of Pure Reason (1781) was, in part, that strategy: by placing the noumenal world permanently beyond the reach of rational knowledge, Kant made claims like Swedenborg’s formally inadmissible in philosophical discourse without needing to refute them. Swedenborg’s book that contains the planetary material most directly is not the Arcana Coelestia but De Telluribus — The Earths in the Universe (1758) — which this close reading covers in full.

“Kant reads all this and then he writes the Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals in a way that develops an abstraction based on Swedenborg… and if you read his earliest writings, which he suppressed in his own lifetime, Kant admits that the reason he did this is that the stuff in Swedenborg is so disturbing that it would destabilise society.”
— Jason Jorjani, American Alchemy episode (see close reading)
◆ The Jorjani thesis in precise form

Jorjani’s argument has three parts: (1) Swedenborg documented direct conversation with beings from other planets — reporting their appearance, speech, consciousness, and spiritual character with first-person specificity. (2) This evidence, if taken seriously, implies that psi abilities (telepathy, clairvoyance, expanded perception) are real and that the universe is populated by non-human intelligence. (3) Kant understood this implication, found it politically and institutionally explosive, and built a philosophical system designed to make such claims permanently unfalsifiable in either direction — not to refute them, but to quarantine them. Whether or not this reading of Kant’s motives is correct, it hinges entirely on whether Swedenborg’s planetary accounts are what Jorjani claims they are. This close reading lets you read those accounts directly.

◊ Kant’s documented engagement

The historical record is clear on one point: Kant did acquire and read all eight volumes of Arcana Coelestia. In 1766 he published Dreams of a Spirit-Seer — nominally debunking Swedenborg but engaging his claims with surprising seriousness, including genuine puzzlement at the internal consistency of the spirit-world accounts. In a private 1763 letter to Charlotte von Knobloch, Kant wrote that he found himself unable to entirely dismiss Swedenborg’s accounts because of the credibility of independent witnesses who reported the same phenomena. Jorjani’s claim about Kant’s suppressed early writings explicitly admitting political motivation is harder to document and contested — but the Kant-Swedenborg entanglement itself is not in dispute.

§1–8The Frame: Why Swedenborg Claims to Know This

The Frame: Why Swedenborg Claims to Know This

📋 Summary

Swedenborg’s opening move is epistemological. He does not claim to have received this information through scripture, revelation, or inference. He claims to have received it empirically — through direct conversation with spirits of the deceased who came from other planets, whom he questioned and interrogated over periods of days, weeks, and months. His framework is precise: all spirits and angels are from the human race; they are human beings who have died and continue to exist near their own earth in the spirit world. Because they retain their full memories of their former lives, they can report on the conditions of their home planets in detail. The mechanism is not mystical in the conventional sense — it is framed as a kind of extended interviewing of posthumous witnesses. The theoretical ground: the universe is too immense to have been created for the inhabitants of one planet alone. The purpose of the cosmos is to generate a heaven populated with souls — and heaven, to be fully constituted, requires a vast number of earths.

“It has been granted me by the Lord to speak and converse with spirits and angels who are from other earths, with some for a day, with some for a week, and with some for months; and to be instructed by them concerning the earths, from which and near which they were; and concerning the lives, customs, and worship of the inhabitants thereof, and of various other things worthy to be related.”
— Swedenborg, §1
“Any man of keen understanding may conclude from many things that he knows that there are many earths, and that there are men there; for it may be concluded from reason that such great masses as the planets are… are not empty masses, and created only to be conveyed in their revolutions round the sun, and to shine with their scanty light for one earth, but that their use must needs be more excellent than that.”
— Swedenborg, §3
◆ The empirical claim that disturbed Kant

Notice that Swedenborg is not making an argument from authority or from scripture. He is making an empirical claim: he interviewed witnesses, cross-referenced their accounts, and reports what he found. This is precisely the structure of a scientific claim, applied to a domain Kant would subsequently declare permanently inaccessible to rational investigation. Jorjani’s argument is that Kant read this and understood its implications: if Swedenborg’s methodology is even partially valid, then psi phenomena are real, non-human intelligences are real, and the post-Enlightenment project of materialism is on very shaky ground.

✓ What is actually verifiable in §1–8

Swedenborg’s astronomical reasoning in §3 is sound: the planets do reflect light rather than emit it (proving they are material bodies), do orbit the sun, do have seasons and days. His conclusion — that they are inhabited — is an inference that is still not definitively settled in either direction for some of them. The claim that has no possible independent verification is that the spirits of those planets came to visit him personally.

§9–45Mercury: The Memory-Keepers Who Despise Matter

Mercury: The Memory-Keepers Who Despise Matter

📋 Summary

Mercury provides the book’s most intellectually striking portrait. The spirits from Mercury are characterised by an overwhelming orientation toward abstract, immaterial knowledge — they are interested exclusively in principles, ideas, governance, and spiritual truths, and actively despise interest in material objects, wealth, or sensory experience. When they visited Swedenborg, they immediately began mining his memory — reading it the way a person reads a book — searching for ideas rather than things. They skipped past his memories of buildings, palaces, and beautiful objects; they wanted governance, customs, the state of learning. Swedenborg identifies Mercury’s role in the Grand Man (the cosmic human being constituted by all of heaven): the spirits of Mercury correspond to the memory of abstract things, as opposed to material things. They wander between solar systems gathering knowledge — the first description of beings who travel across interstellar distances out of intellectual curiosity. Communication: largely telepathic, reading human memory directly without being asked.

“They said they had no delight in looking at things material, corporeal, and terrestrial, but only at things real… When they looked into those things which I knew respecting heavenly things, they passed hastily through them all, continually saying that this and that were so and so… they read the things contained therein, as out of a book.”
— Swedenborg, §11–13
“These spirits are permitted to wander about, and even to pass out of this solar system into others, and to procure for themselves knowledges. They have declared that there are earths inhabited by men, not only in this solar system, but also out of it in the starry heaven, to an immense number.”
— Swedenborg, §6
☾ Appearance

Appear in the world of spirits in the direction corresponding to Mercury’s position. Not described physically in detail. Known primarily through their mode of engagement: rapid, extractive, intellectually voracious.

💬 Communication

Telepathic extraction of memory. They enter a person’s memory and read it directly — faster and more efficiently than reading text, because they skip material concerns entirely.

★ Role in the Grand Man

Correspond to memory of abstracted things — the abstract intellectual faculty of the cosmic organism. Their counterparts from Venus correspond to material memory, and together they form a complementary pair.

🌎 Notable claim

The only beings in the book described as travelling between solar systems. They do this habitually, out of intellectual curiosity alone, having confirmed that inhabited earths exist throughout the starry heaven.

◆ Why this is the chapter Jorjani has in mind

The Mercury account is the most directly UAP-relevant section in Swedenborg’s corpus. Beings who: travel between solar systems; communicate telepathically by directly reading another being’s memory; are entirely uninterested in material reality but intensely interested in consciousness, governance, and spiritual truth; and who confirm by first-hand interstellar travel that the universe is full of inhabited worlds. This is structurally identical to certain categories of reported contact experience in the modern UAP literature — including accounts of telepathic information transfer and contempt for material concerns among some reported entities. Whether this is convergent mythology, a real phenomenon Swedenborg accessed, or extraordinary coincidence is precisely the question Kant’s philosophical framework makes formally unaskable.

§46–84Jupiter: The Gentle Giants and the Golden Age Template

Jupiter: The Gentle Giants and the Golden Age Template

📋 Summary

Jupiter receives the longest treatment in the book — Swedenborg reports spending more time conversing with Jovian spirits than with those of any other world. The portrait is idealised: the Jupiterians live in what Swedenborg explicitly compares to the ancient “Golden Age” and “Saturnian age” described by classical writers — organised by nations, families, and houses rather than kingdoms; never coveting the possessions of others; living by the laws of charity inscribed on their hearts rather than by external legislation. Their communication with Swedenborg produced a “gentleness and sweetness” of influx unlike anything he had experienced from earthly spirits. They appear to him with cheerful, beautiful faces — larger than earthly faces, they believe — and their spiritual influx made him feel his breast and heart “very sensibly” filled with tranquillity. Most striking: their interiors are described as “open to heaven” in a way that earthly humans’ are not, making them capable of receiving divine good directly. They consider war, depredation, and murder so horrific they refuse to hear about these practices when Swedenborg describes Earth.

“When I would have told them that on this earth there are wars, depredations, and murders, they then turned away, and were unwilling to hear… It was declared to me by the angels that the most ancient people on this earth lived in like manner, namely, that they were distinguished into nations, families, and houses; that all at that time were content with their own possessions; that it was a thing altogether unknown for one person to enrich himself from the goods of another.”
— Swedenborg, §49
“I could distinguish the presence of the spirits of Jupiter… from this, that for the most part their influx was into the face, and made it smiling and cheerful… That tranquillity and delight with which they inspired me filled my breast and heart very sensibly; at the same time cupidities and anxieties concerning things to come were removed.”
— Swedenborg, §51
✓ The Golden Age parallel

Swedenborg’s explicit comparison of Jupiter to humanity’s “Golden Age” is significant: he is claiming that the Jovians currently live in the social and spiritual condition that Earth’s most ancient people enjoyed and then lost. This maps onto his larger historical theology (the Most Ancient Church was of the “celestial” type, characterised by direct perception from love) — but applies it cosmologically: Earth is a spiritually degraded planet by comparison with Jupiter. The implication for the question of ET intelligence: advanced civilisations may be morally and spiritually superior, not just technologically.

◊ What Swedenborg observed about faces

The detail about Jovian faces being larger and rounder than earthly faces — a belief the inhabitants hold and which therefore shapes their spirit-world appearance — is one of the book’s peculiar specifics. Swedenborg explains the mechanism: because spirits appear in the form their belief and expectation creates, the Jovians’ cultural conviction that they will have larger faces in the afterlife produces that appearance. This is consistent with his broader epistemology — appearance in the spirit world is determined by interior state — but the detail is unusual enough to be memorable.

§85–96Mars: The Celestial Telepaths

Mars: The Celestial Telepaths

📋 Summary

The Mars chapter is the most technically specific on the question of communication. Martian spirits communicate through an entirely non-verbal, almost tacit speech — breathing rather than vocalising, entering the mind through what Swedenborg describes as the Eustachian tube rather than the eardrum. When they first contacted him, he could not understand their communication because it operated below audible sound — it was “like the softest aura,” felt rather than heard, flowing from his left temple through his left eye and finally into his brain. Only then could he understand it. Crucially, Swedenborg identifies this as the mode of communication of the Most Ancient Church on Earth — pre-Flood humanity — who spoke by internal respiration from the navel toward the heart, not by external vocalisation. The Martians are currently living in the spiritual condition Earth’s earliest humans once had. They are described as “the best of all among the spirits from the earths of our solar system” — specifically because they most closely resemble the celestial type. Their defining characteristic: they regard it as “nefarious” to think one thing and say another. Their thought and their expression are a single unified event.

“Spirits from Mars came to me and applied themselves to my left temple, where they breathed upon me with their speech; but I did not understand it. It was soft in its flow, softer I had never before perceived; it was like the softest aura… when the breathing arrived there, I then understood their speech.”
— Swedenborg, §87
“They regard it as nefarious to think one thing and speak another, and to will one thing and show another in the face. They do not know what hypocrisy is, nor what fraudulent pretence and deceit are.”
— Swedenborg, §87
“The spirits of Mars are the best of all among the spirits who are from the earths of our solar system, for they are as to the most part celestial men, not unlike those who were of the most ancient church on this earth.”
— Swedenborg, §85
💬 Speech mode

Non-vocal, non-sonorous. Transmitted by breathing rather than vocalisation. Enters through the Eustachian tube rather than the eardrum. Felt in the body before it is understood by the mind.

★ Spiritual grade

“Best of all in our solar system.” Celestial type — the highest level in Swedenborg’s hierarchy, characterised by perception directly from love rather than faith.

☾ Defining characteristic

Thought and expression are unified: they cannot dissemble. The face and eyes automatically express what is inwardly thought and felt. Hypocrisy is literally impossible for them.

◆ Historical parallel

Their speech mode is identical to that of Earth’s Most Ancient Church. Mars currently inhabits Earth’s lost spiritual condition.

◆ The Mars chapter in the UAP context

The Martian communication mode — non-verbal, felt in the body before being understood, bypassing the external auditory apparatus — maps structurally onto descriptions of telepathic contact in modern contact narratives and some UFO abduction accounts. The impossibility of deception (thought and expression unified) is also a recurring feature in reports of highly advanced entities. Whether Swedenborg was accessing a real phenomenon, projecting an idealised psychology, or creating a template that later contactees unconsciously reproduced is genuinely open. What is not open is that the account is strikingly specific: not just “they communicated without words” but a precise description of which physical pathway the communication used and which faculty received it.

§97–112Saturn, Venus, and the Moon

Saturn, Venus, and the Moon

📋 Summary

Three shorter portraits. Saturn (§97–104): The Saturnian spirits are exceptionally humble — they account themselves as nothing in the presence of the divine. They worship the Lord as the only God; when spirits from Earth try to lead them away from this worship, they would rather die than be drawn away. They carry small knives and seem to threaten their own lives — but this is explained as a spiritual appearance, not a literal act: the will of their mind to die rather than apostatise becomes visible. A striking detail: they report that other spirits (curious, question-asking strangers) frequently visit them and try to extract information from their minds. Venus (§105–110): Two opposite types on the same planet — mild and humane on the far side, savage and bestial on the near side. The savage Venusians are “giants” whose men reach only to the navels of earthly humans. They delight in plunder and feasting on what they steal; they are “stupid,” making no inquiries about heaven or eternal life. The Moon (§111–112): The most unusual portrait. The lunar spirits are dwarfs — the size of seven-year-old boys but more robust. They communicate by thundering — a booming voice produced from the abdomen rather than the lungs, because the Moon lacks a proper atmosphere. Two of them arrived by one riding on the other’s back.

“They said that sometimes spirits from our earth come to them, and ask them what God they worship; to whom they reply, that they are insane, and that there cannot be a greater proof of insanity than to ask what God anyone worships, when there is but one only God for all in the universe.”
— Swedenborg, §99 — Saturn
“The lunar spirits… appeared not unhandsome, but longer than the faces of other spirits. Their stature was like the stature of boys seven years old, but more robust; thus they were dwarfs… He who was carried on the other’s back… applied himself to my left side under the elbow, and thence spoke with me, saying that whenever they utter their voices they thus thunder.”
— Swedenborg, §111 — Moon
◊ The Moon’s atmosphere

Swedenborg’s explanation for the lunar spirits’ abdominal speech — that the Moon lacks a proper atmosphere — is presented as a consequence of the correspondence principle: inhabitants adapt their physical communication to the conditions of their world. This is, incidentally, consistent with what we know about the Moon (it does lack a conventional atmosphere), though Swedenborg had no way to verify this observationally in 1758 and frames it as spirit-world testimony.

§113–122Why the Lord Was Born on Earth and Not Another Planet

Why the Lord Was Born on Earth and Not Another Planet

📋 Summary

This is the book’s theological centrepiece — and the section most relevant to Jorjani’s argument about why this material disturbed Kant. Swedenborg poses a direct question: given that there are innumerable inhabited worlds, why did the Lord choose to incarnate specifically on Earth? His answer has several components that bear directly on Earth’s spiritual status relative to the other worlds he has described. Earth is not chosen because of spiritual superiority — in fact, Swedenborg explicitly states that Earth’s inhabitants are among the worst in the universe in terms of spiritual condition. Earth was chosen partly because the Word (scripture) was given here, allowing the Lord’s incarnation to be transmitted to other worlds through the spiritual correspondence network. The other worlds receive knowledge of the Lord not through their own incarnation but through communication with spirits who learned it from Earth. This section establishes the cosmic function of Earth in Swedenborg’s system: not a privileged planet but a necessary node — the place where the correspondence between the Divine and the Natural was most fully broken, and therefore the place where it needed to be most fully repaired.

“The reason that our Lord was born on this earth, and not on another, is: because on this earth the Word could be written, and being written, could be published through the whole earth, and, once published, could be preserved to all posterity… In other earths, there is no such thing as printing, and so the Word cannot be preserved by writings, nor extended to so many people.”
— Swedenborg, §113 paraphrase
◆ The cosmic communications network

This section reveals the structure underlying the whole book: all inhabited worlds are connected through the spirit world’s correspondence network. Spirits from Mercury travel between solar systems sharing knowledge. The Lord’s incarnation on Earth radiates to other worlds through this network. The Grand Man — the cosmic human body constituted by all of heaven — requires contributions from spirits of many worlds to be fully constituted. What Swedenborg is describing is an interstellar spiritual infrastructure in which Earth is one node among innumerable others. This is the picture that, Jorjani argues, Kant found too politically explosive to leave standing as a legitimate philosophical claim.

✓ Earth’s spiritual status in comparative context

Swedenborg’s hierarchy, assembled across the planetary chapters, is stark: Mars best, then Jupiter, then Saturn (humble), then Mercury (intellectually oriented), then Venus (mixed), then Moon (strange), then Earth — described as among the spiritually worst planets in our solar system. The reason Earth was chosen for the Incarnation is not its virtue but the specific function of the Word as a communicable written record. This is an unusually self-deprecating cosmological claim for a Christian theologian to make.

§123–178The Earths in the Starry Heaven: Interstellar Journeys

The Earths in the Starry Heaven: Interstellar Journeys

📋 Summary

The final and most extraordinary section. Swedenborg reports being transported — as to his spirit, his body remaining in place — to five different earths orbiting stars outside our solar system. The journey mechanism is described precisely: it is not travel through physical space but travel through “changes of state of the interiors” — the spirit world is organised by similarity of inner states rather than spatial distance, so approaching a distant world means shifting one’s inner condition to match those who live there. The first journey took two hours; another took two days. He is accompanied by spirits from Earth. At each destination he finds spirits of the deceased inhabitants waiting near their world and interviews them. First earth: an immense world whose spirits appear of gigantic size, whose inhabitants live up to 300 years, communicate by expression and gesture rather than speech, and worship the Lord in human form. Second earth: spirits of exceptional perceptual acuity (“keenness of vision”) who appear at great heights above him — they can see everything beneath them. Troubled by Earth’s missionaries who have been attempting to disturb their faith. Fifth earth: inhabitants who have been badgered by wandering spirits (identified as Catholic missionaries) trying to introduce the concept of a Trinity and an invisible God, which these beings find incoherent because they perceive God directly in human form.

“I was borne across the middle of it; and then there appeared beneath in that chasm or gulf very many things, like glowing coals and firebrands interspersed with small flames; being dense vapors from that great chasm, which were kindled there by the light of our sun… After the transit of this, I was led to an earth in the starry heaven.”
— Swedenborg, §128
“They were afraid we were of those who disturb them in regard to God, to faith, and things of a like nature… We asked them by what they were disturbed. They replied, by an idea of three, and by an idea of the Divine without the human, in God, when yet they know and perceive that God is one, and that he is man.”
— Swedenborg, §169 — Fifth earth
“They said further, that the above spirits confuse them by asserting that they ought to have faith and to believe the things they say; but they replied to them, that they know not what faith is nor what is meant by believing, since they perceive it.”
— Swedenborg, §169
◆ The most important detail in the starry heaven section

The fifth earth’s inhabitants say they “know not what faith is nor what is meant by believing, since they perceive it.” This is the key distinction from Swedenborg’s cosmology applied interstellarly: these beings are in the “celestial” state — direct perception of divine truth from love, without need of faith as intermediary — which Swedenborg identifies as the condition of Earth’s most ancient humans before the Fall. The Catholic missionaries’ insistence on faith (belief without perception) is experienced by these beings as incoherent noise. Jorjani’s point: if Swedenborg’s accounts are even partially valid, then the universe contains beings in a state of consciousness that humans once had and lost — and the philosophical tradition from Kant onward has systematically made it impossible to discuss this possibility seriously.

◊ The mechanism of interstellar travel

Swedenborg’s travel mechanism — changes of interior state rather than movement through space — is the most sophisticated aspect of the book philosophically. He is describing consciousness-based access to distant locations: the spirit world is organised by similarity rather than distance, so one does not travel to another star system by covering the intervening void but by shifting one’s inner state to align with those who are there. This maps onto certain modern descriptions of non-local consciousness and onto some accounts of out-of-body experience. Whether it maps onto anything real is precisely what Kant’s critical philosophy makes impossible to investigate through normal epistemic channels.

AssessmentWhat to Make of This: Swedenborg vs. Kant vs. Jorjani

What to Make of This: Swedenborg vs. Kant vs. Jorjani

📋 Summary

The book poses three distinct questions that are often conflated. First: is the content true? That is, did Swedenborg actually communicate with beings from other planets? Second: is the content internally consistent and coherent? That is, does the system hold together on its own terms? Third: was Kant’s response to this material what Jorjani claims — a philosophically motivated suppression rather than a straightforward dismissal? These three questions have very different answers.

◊ On the first question: is it true?

There is no independent verification of any of Swedenborg’s specific planetary claims — we cannot interview spirits from Mars or Mercury. What can be noted: (1) Some of his astronomical observations are accurate — the planets are material bodies, they orbit the sun, some have moons. (2) The psychological and social descriptions of each world are internally consistent across his many volumes — the Mercury account in Arcana Coelestia matches the account here. (3) Some details are falsified — the Moon, we know, is uninhabited, and Venus has two entirely opposite human populations by Swedenborg’s account which does not match reality. (4) The communication modes he describes (particularly for Mars) are strikingly specific in ways that do not obviously derive from his prior theological commitments.

✓ On the second question: internal coherence

The system is remarkably coherent. Each planet’s spiritual character is consistent with its role in the Grand Man; each communication mode reflects that world’s spiritual level; each world’s social organisation reflects where its inhabitants sit on the celestial-spiritual-natural spectrum. The interstellar journey mechanism (changes of inner state rather than spatial travel) is philosophically sophisticated and consistent with Swedenborg’s general metaphysics. Whatever else this is, it is not random or improvised.

◆ On the third question: was Kant suppressing this?

This is where Jorjani’s reading is most provocative and most contested. The documented facts: Kant read all eight volumes of Arcana Coelestia; found Swedenborg’s accounts harder to dismiss than he expected; published a careful ambivalent critique; and then, fifteen years later, published the first Critique — which systematically closes off the possibility of knowledge claims about any non-empirical domain. What Jorjani adds — that Kant’s suppressed early writings admit this was politically motivated — requires documentation that is harder to pin down. The structural argument, however, stands independently: Kant’s Critical philosophy does function as a philosophical containment system for exactly the kind of claim Swedenborg makes, regardless of whether that was its intended purpose.
