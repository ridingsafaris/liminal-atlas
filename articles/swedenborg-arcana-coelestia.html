Emanuel Swedenborg —
Arcana Coelestia

Author: Emanuel Swedenborg (1688–1772) Full title: Heavenly Arcana Published: London, 1749–1756 (8 vols.) This volume: Genesis 1–9, §1–1113 649 pages · Swedenborg Foundation edition

Arcana Coelestia is the work Immanuel Kant read in full and then built a philosophy to contain. Published anonymously in Latin at Swedenborg’s own expense between 1749 and 1756, its eight volumes offer a verse-by-verse internal-sense commentary on Genesis and Exodus, interspersed with first-person accounts of the author’s travels through the spirit world — heaven, hell, and the intermediate state. The method is correspondence: every word of scripture encodes a precise spiritual meaning through a structural relationship between the natural and spiritual worlds that Swedenborg claims to have directly observed. The result is one of the most systematic mystical theologies in Western literature, and one of the most ignored.

📋 About this close reading

This reads Volume 1 of Arcana Coelestia (covering Genesis 1–9, sections 1–1113) section by section, tracing Swedenborg’s correspondence system as he applies it, quoting his first-person spirit-world accounts, and placing his claims in the context of their historical reception. Green boxes surface the internal logic and genuine originality of his system. Amber boxes flag connections to the wider intellectual tradition. The Kant connection is treated as a live question, not a settled verdict.

⚠ Where are the planets and aliens?

They are not in this volume. Volume 1 of Arcana Coelestia contains no discussion of inhabitants of other planets, spirits from Mercury or Jupiter, extraterrestrial intelligences, or UFO-adjacent phenomena. That material is in two different places:

1. Later volumes of Arcana Coelestia (vols. 5–8). As the commentary progresses through Exodus, Swedenborg inserts long continuation essays reporting conversations with spirits who identified themselves as inhabitants of other planets in our solar system — Mercury, Jupiter, Mars, Saturn, Venus, the Moon — and from worlds orbiting other suns. He describes their appearance, their spiritual character, their religious practices, and the way they communicate (many telepathically, without spoken language). These sections in the later volumes are the material that disturbed Kant.

2. The Earths in the Universe (De Telluribus, 1758). Swedenborg extracted and expanded this planetary material into a standalone work published two years after the final volume of Arcana Coelestia. This is the dedicated book on spirits from other worlds — the one most relevant to the Jorjani discussion about why Kant found the corpus so alarming. It describes in systematic detail the spirits of each planet, their physical and spiritual characteristics, and their relationship to the Grand Man of heaven. The book is short (under 200 pages) and the most directly “UFO-relevant” of Swedenborg’s works.

Volume 1, covered in this close reading, is the foundation: the correspondence system, the cosmological framework, and the method of internal-sense reading that makes the later planetary accounts coherent within his system. Without understanding how Swedenborg reads “heaven” and “earth” and “spirits,” the planetary material looks merely eccentric. With it, it becomes a consistent extension of his first principles.

Author Emanuel Swedenborg (1688–1772)

Emanuel Swedenborg (1688–1772)

📋 Summary

Emanuel Swedenborg was Sweden’s most distinguished natural scientist before becoming, at age 56, a visionary theologian who claimed direct access to the spirit world. Born in Stockholm, son of a bishop, he mastered mathematics, engineering, anatomy, metallurgy, astronomy, and physics — publishing landmark work on crystallography, the nebular hypothesis, and cerebral anatomy. His mechanical engineering feats included designing a fourteen-metre wooden submarine and supervising the overland transport of an entire Swedish naval fleet across a mountain range. In 1744–45, in his mid-fifties, a crisis occurred: vivid dreams, visions, and what he described as an awakening of his spiritual senses. He claimed thereafter to live simultaneously in the material world and the world of spirits — able to converse with angels and the deceased at will, walking through the halls of heaven and the chambers of hell as a first-person observer. He spent the final twenty-seven years of his life publishing twelve volumes of theology in Latin, entirely at his own expense.

“It has been granted me now for some years to be constantly and uninterruptedly in company with spirits and angels, hearing them speak and in turn speaking with them. In this way it has been given me to hear and see wonderful things in the other life which have never before come to the knowledge of any man, nor into his idea.”
— Swedenborg, Arcana Coelestia §5
◊ Why Kant read him — and what he suppressed

In 1766 Immanuel Kant published Dreams of a Spirit-Seer — nominally a debunking of Swedenborg, but one that engaged his claims with unusual seriousness. As Jason Jorjani argues in Prometheus and Atlas (discussed in Liminal Atlas’s close reading of his American Alchemy episode), Kant had read the entirety of Arcana Coelestia and found the accounts of ESP and extraterrestrial intelligences deeply disturbing — disturbing enough to motivate his construction of the “unknowable noumenal realm,” a philosophical move that walled off such claims from rational discourse. Kant’s early suppressed writings reportedly admit this political motivation explicitly. Whether or not that reading of Kant is correct, the documented fact remains: Kant read all eight volumes of Arcana Coelestia and found it impossible to dismiss in private, whatever he said in public.

Method The Internal Sense: How Swedenborg Reads Scripture

The Internal Sense: How Swedenborg Reads Scripture

📋 Summary

The entire edifice of Arcana Coelestia rests on a single hermeneutical claim: every word of Scripture, down to “the most minute iota,” has a literal sense visible to ordinary reading and an internal sense that only the spiritually illuminated can read. The literal sense concerns Jewish history, rites, and ordinances. The internal sense concerns the Lord, the states of the human soul, and the process of spiritual regeneration. These two senses are not loosely parallel — Swedenborg claims a precise, consistent symbolic vocabulary (called correspondence) linking every natural object to its spiritual counterpart. The “heavens” of Genesis 1 correspond to the internal mind. The “earth” corresponds to the external. Light is understanding of truth. Darkness is ignorance. Water is knowledge at the natural level. This system is not metaphor — Swedenborg insists it is the actual structure of creation, the fabric of Divine design: that God created the natural world as a continuous expression of the spiritual world, so that every natural thing is a living symbol of its spiritual cause.

“From the mere letter of the Word of the Old Testament no one would ever discern the fact that this part of the Word contains deep secrets of heaven, and that everything within it both in general and in particular bears reference to the Lord, to his heaven, to the church, to religious belief, and to all things connected therewith.”
— Arcana Coelestia §1
“Without such a life, the Word as to the letter is dead. The case in this respect is the same as it is with man, who is both internal and external. When separated from the internal man, the external man is the body, and is therefore dead; for it is the internal man that is alive and that causes the external man to be so, the internal man being the soul. So is it with the Word, which, in respect to the letter alone, is like the body without the soul.”
— Arcana Coelestia §3
◆ The structure of each chapter

Each of Swedenborg’s Genesis commentaries follows a strict three-part form: (1) the biblical verses are quoted verbatim; (2) The Contents — a brief outline of the internal meaning of each verse; (3) The Internal Sense — a detailed verse-by-verse exposition decoding each word through the correspondence system. Between Genesis chapters, Swedenborg inserts independent continuation essays on what he witnessed during his spirit journeys — accounts of heaven, hell, and the nature of souls after death. These continuations are arguably the most extraordinary and most read parts of the work.

◊ The claim of direct revelation

Swedenborg’s hermeneutical authority rests entirely on his claim that the internal sense was revealed to him directly, not derived. He is not proposing an allegorical reading — he claims that the correspondences are objectively real, that he confirmed them by direct observation in the spiritual world, and that his conversations with angels verified the system. This is an empirical claim of a very unusual kind: the correspondence system is offered as heaven’s own testimony, delivered across the veil.

Genesis 1 The Six Days: Stages of Spiritual Regeneration

The Six Days: Stages of Spiritual Regeneration

📋 Summary

Genesis 1 — the creation account — is not, in Swedenborg’s reading, about the creation of the physical universe. It is about the regeneration of the human soul, described allegorically under the figure of creation. The six days are six successive stages through which a spiritually dead person passes on the way to becoming, first, a “spiritual man” (who acts from faith), and then a “celestial man” (who acts from love, which is a higher and more direct state). The seventh day — the sabbath — is not rest but the completed state of celestial man, in whom the Lord rests because love governs without conflict. Swedenborg identifies three kinds of human being in this progression: the dead man (who acknowledges only the material world), the spiritual man (who acts from faith, still in internal combat), and the celestial man (who perceives divine truth directly from love, without need of faith as an intermediary). The Most Ancient Church — the pre-flood humanity — was of this celestial type: they perceived divine truth immediately, the way one perceives the warmth of the sun, not through doctrines but through direct spiritual sensation called perception.

“The six days, or periods, which are so many successive states of the regeneration of man, are in general as follows: The first state is that which precedes, including both the state from infancy, and that immediately before regeneration. This is called a ‘void,’ ‘emptiness,’ and ‘thick darkness.’”
— Arcana Coelestia §6–7
“The sixth state is when, from faith, and thence from love, he speaks what is true, and does what is good… he becomes a spiritual man, who is called an ‘image.’ His spiritual life is delighted and sustained by such things as belong to the knowledges of faith, and to works of charity… and his natural life by those which belong to the body and the senses; whence a combat arises, until love gains the dominion, and he becomes a celestial man.”
— Arcana Coelestia §12
◆ The correspondence table for Genesis 1

Void and emptiness = the unregenerate soul, empty of spiritual life. Light = first understanding of truth. Firmament = rational faculty separating higher from lower. Dry land = the external man becoming ordered. Plants = first moral goods from knowledge. Two luminaries = love (sun) and faith (moon). Fish and birds = sensory and rational knowledges animated by faith. Living soul / beast = affections now governed by charity. Man in God’s image = the spiritual man, in whom internal and external cooperate. The correspondence system means these mappings are not invented — Swedenborg claims they are the actual language heaven uses.

Genesis 2 Eden: The Celestial Man and the Two Trees

Eden: The Celestial Man and the Two Trees

📋 Summary

Genesis 2 is not a second creation account but a description of the state of the Most Ancient Church — the pre-Flood humanity who inhabited the celestial level of spiritual life. In this state, man perceives truth directly from love, without needing faith as intermediary. The Garden of Eden is the mind of this celestial man: “heaven” is his internal mind, “earth” is his external mind. The trees of the garden are his perceptions — each tree a distinct form of spiritual understanding. The Tree of Life at the garden’s centre represents love itself — the Lord’s presence within the soul. The Tree of Knowledge of Good and Evil represents the alternative way of knowing: through sensory evidence and self-derived reason rather than through divinely implanted perception. The four rivers flowing from Eden represent wisdom (the river itself), knowledge of good and truth (Pishon/Gihon, flowing through the internal man), reason (Hiddekel), and sensory memory-knowledge (Euphrates, the outermost). Adam and Eve are not historical individuals but symbolic representations: Adam = the rational faculty, Eve = the love of the will, together constituting the complete celestial human being.

“By ‘heaven’ is meant his internal man, and ‘earth’ his external; ‘the army of them’ are love, faith, and the knowledges thereof… A celestial man believes and perceives spiritual and celestial truth and good, acknowledging no other faith than that which is from love, from which also he acts.”
— Arcana Coelestia §81–82
“The celestial man is not in combat, and when assaulted by evils and falsities, he despises them, and is therefore called a conqueror. He is apparently restrained by no bonds, but is free. His bonds, which are not apparent, are perceptions of good and truth.”
— Arcana Coelestia §81
◆ The crucial distinction: perception vs. faith

Swedenborg draws a sharp distinction between the celestial man’s perception and the spiritual man’s faith. The celestial man perceives divine truth directly — he does not need to believe it because he senses it, the way the eye senses light without needing to infer it. Faith is for those who cannot yet perceive directly — it is a lower form of knowing, appropriate for the spiritual man in the current age, but not the final state. The Most Ancient Church had perception. Post-flood humanity largely operates on faith. The loss of perception is, for Swedenborg, the central spiritual tragedy of human history — the Fall’s actual meaning.

Genesis 3 The Fall: When Sense-Experience Overthrew Perception

The Fall: When Sense-Experience Overthrew Perception

📋 Summary

The serpent of Genesis 3 is not Satan in any conventional sense. Swedenborg identifies it as the “sensuous part of man” — the faculty that trusts only what it can see, feel, and verify through physical evidence. The serpent is called “more subtle than any wild animal” because the sensuous faculty is the cleverest of all man’s faculties: it reasons brilliantly from empirical evidence, but it reasons about the wrong things and from the wrong ground. The woman represents “man’s own” — the self-derived will, the love of one’s own ideas and impulses rather than divine perception. The man represents the rational faculty. The serpent persuades the woman — the self-love — to begin testing divine truth against sensory evidence: “yea, hath God said?” is the voice of empiricism applied to spiritual matters. The Fall, in this reading, is the moment when humanity began to require sense-evidence before accepting spiritual truth — when direct celestial perception was replaced by the need to verify. This is not a moral failure but an epistemological one: the substitution of sensory rationalism for direct spiritual cognition. The death God threatened was not physical death but the death of celestial perception.

“By the ‘serpent’ is here meant the sensuous part of man in which he trusts; by the ‘wild animal of the field,’ every affection of the external man; by the ‘woman,’ man’s own [proprium]. The subject here treated of is the third posterity of the most ancient church, which began not to believe in things revealed unless they saw and felt that they were so.”
— Arcana Coelestia §194
“The sensuous things in man they called ‘serpents,’ because as serpents live close to the earth, so sensuous things are those next the body. Hence also reasonings concerning the mysteries of faith, founded on the evidence of the senses, were called by them the ‘poison of a serpent,’ and the reasoners themselves ‘serpents.’”
— Arcana Coelestia §195
◆ The Fall as an epistemological rather than moral event

This is one of Swedenborg’s most striking rereadings. The conventional understanding of the Fall as moral disobedience is replaced by an account of a cognitive shift: humanity began requiring empirical verification for spiritual truths. The serpent is not evil but epistemologically mistaken — it is the faculty that produces natural science. Swedenborg is not anti-reason, but he is insisting that reason applied to spiritual questions from a sensory base (“does God exist? show me evidence”) is precisely the wrong tool — the serpent’s method. The celestial church did not reason their way to God; they perceived him directly, the way one perceives warmth. When this perception failed, faith became necessary — and when faith weakens, only empirical-style evidence will serve, which is insufficient for its purpose.

Genesis 4 Cain and Abel: The Doctrine of Faith Alone vs. Charity

Cain and Abel: The Doctrine of Faith Alone vs. Charity

📋 Summary

Cain and Abel represent two doctrinal positions that split from the original celestial unity. Abel represents charity — love toward the neighbour, which is the form love of God takes in human life. Cain represents faith separated from charity — the doctrine that correct belief, without love, constitutes the substance of religion. The murder of Abel by Cain is the historical defeat of the charity-centred church by the doctrine of faith-alone. Jehovah’s acceptance of Abel’s offering and rejection of Cain’s is not arbitrary: the offering of charity (love in action) is acceptable to the Lord because love is the Lord’s own substance; the offering of separated faith, however doctrinally correct, misses the living core. The “mark on Cain” — usually read as curse or stigma — is in Swedenborg’s reading a sign of protection: the separated-faith tradition, though corrupt, still contains vestiges of truth that should not be destroyed, because they can eventually lead back to genuine understanding. Seth, born to replace Abel, represents a new church that restores the combination of faith and charity, called “Enosh” after Seth’s son.

“The most ancient church had faith in the Lord through love; but there arose some who separated faith from love. The doctrine of faith separated from love was called ‘Cain’; and charity, which is love toward the neighbor, was called ‘Abel.’”
— Arcana Coelestia §325
◊ The Lutheran resonance

Swedenborg’s reading of Cain as the “faith alone” (sola fide) doctrine is a pointed critique of Lutheran Protestantism from within the Lutheran tradition. Luther’s great reformation principle — justification by faith alone, not works — is, in Swedenborg’s allegorical reading, the very error Genesis 4 is warning against. Swedenborg does not name Luther here, but his later theological works make this critique explicit. The New Church he inspired was, among other things, a reaction against what he saw as Protestantism’s fatal severance of faith from love.

◆ The structure of the continuation essays: the soul after death

Between the Genesis 4 exegesis and Genesis 5, Swedenborg inserts his first substantial first-person account of the afterlife — describing the process by which a soul transitions from the body to the spirit world. He recounts being “reduced into a state of insensibility as to the bodily senses” while retaining his interior life, then observing celestial angels occupying “the region of the heart,” two angels sitting at his head who communicated thoughts by means of facial expression rather than words, and the gradual process by which a soul awakens to its new condition. These accounts — reported as first-person experiences in the present tense — are what made Swedenborg’s work impossible for contemporaries to simply ignore.

Genesis 5 + Continuation The Ten Patriarchs as Ten Churches; Heaven & Heavenly Joy

The Ten Patriarchs as Ten Churches; Heaven & Heavenly Joy

📋 Summary

Genesis 5’s long genealogy — ten patriarchs from Adam to Noah, each living hundreds of years — is not a record of individual people. Each patriarch names a successive church state or spiritual epoch of pre-Flood humanity: Seth, Enosh, Kenan, Mahalalel, Jared, Enoch, Methuselah, Lamech, Noah. Each represents a distinct quality of the Most Ancient Church as it developed, changed, and eventually deteriorated. The figure of Enoch — who “walked with God and was no more, for God took him” — represents the church that preserved its celestial doctrine in written form for the benefit of later ages: “framing doctrine from what was revealed to and perceived by the most ancient church, which doctrine, although of no use at that time, was preserved for the use of posterity.” Noah, the tenth, represents the transition: not the Most Ancient Church itself but the seed of a new kind of spiritual life that would survive the “flood” — a complete restructuring of the human psyche. The continuation essay inserted here is Swedenborg’s richest account of heavenly society: three heavens (good spirits, angelic spirits, angels), each divided into innumerable societies organised by degree and variety of love, constituting together “one man” — the Grand Man (Maximus Homo), the cosmic human being whose body is formed by the entire population of heaven.

“There are three heavens: the first is the abode of good spirits, the second of angelic spirits, and the third of angels. Each heaven is distinguished into innumerable societies; and each society consists of many individuals, who by their harmony and unanimity constitute as it were one person; and all the societies together are as one man.”
— Arcana Coelestia §684
“The souls who come into the other life are all ignorant of the nature of heaven and of heavenly joy. Very many suppose it to be a kind of joy into which any can be admitted no matter how they have lived… quite unaware of the fact that heaven is mutual and chaste love, and that heavenly joy is the derivative happiness.”
— Arcana Coelestia §547
◆ The Grand Man (Maximus Homo)

Swedenborg’s most original cosmological idea: heaven is structured as a perfect human being. Each society of heaven corresponds to an organ or region of the body — societies near the heart radiate love, those near the lungs wisdom in articulate form, those near the eyes provide overview, those near the hands provide power. This is not metaphor but literal anatomy: the correspondence system that governs nature also governs heaven’s organisation. This concept of the Maximus Homo influenced later thought on the relationship between the individual soul, the social body, and the cosmos, appearing in various forms in Blake, Coleridge, and 19th-century idealist philosophy.

Genesis 6 The Nephilim, the Sons of God, and the Approach of the Flood

The Nephilim, the Sons of God, and the Approach of the Flood

📋 Summary

Genesis 6 — the passage where “the sons of God saw the daughters of men and took wives” and the Nephilim walked the earth — is among the most contested in all of scripture. Swedenborg’s reading is psychological: the “sons of God” are the doctrinal truths of faith; the “daughters of man” are sensory yearnings and self-loves. The intermarriage of the two means the corruption of spiritual doctrine by being merged with bodily desire — using the language of faith to justify and empower self-gratification. The Nephilim (“mighty men of old, men of renown”) are those who combined doctrinal knowledge with enormous self-love, producing “direful persuasions” — the most spiritually dangerous state, in which a person is absolutely certain of their own divine authority and righteousness. This state cannot be corrected because correction requires residual openness to truth (what Swedenborg calls “remains”), and the Nephilim have consumed their remains in self-worship. The Flood becomes necessary not as divine punishment but as the only possible resolution: this spiritual state is literally unsurvivable, like a biological organism that has destroyed the conditions of its own life. Importantly, Swedenborg defines remains here — one of his most important concepts: the preserved deposits of innocence, love, and genuine goodness stored in the interior of the soul from childhood onward, laid down by the Lord without the person’s knowledge, and essential to any possibility of regeneration.

“But what are remains? They are not only the goods and truths that a man has learned from the Lord’s Word from infancy, and has thus impressed on his memory, but they are also all the states thence derived, such as states of innocence from infancy; states of love toward parents, brothers, teachers, friends; states of charity toward the neighbor, and also of pity for the poor and needy… All these states together with the goods and truths impressed on the memory, are called remains, which are preserved in man by the Lord, entirely without his knowledge.”
— Arcana Coelestia §561
“The people before the flood were such that at last they had almost no remains, because they were of such a genius that they became imbued with direful and abominable persuasions concerning all things that occurred to them or came into their thought, so that they would not go back from them one whit, for they were possessed with the most enormous love of self, and supposed themselves to be as gods.”
— Arcana Coelestia §562
◊ Swedenborg’s Nephilim vs. the ancient astronaut tradition

The “sons of God” and Nephilim passage has generated enormous interpretive controversy, including the modern “ancient astronaut” reading (extraterrestrial beings interbreeding with humanity) popularised by Sitchin and referenced by Bramley in The Gods of Eden. Swedenborg’s reading is entirely psychological — the “sons of God” are not beings from elsewhere but the doctrinal component of the human soul. His exegesis makes no room for literal angelic-human crossbreeding; the text’s meaning operates entirely at the level of the soul’s internal states.

Genesis 7–8 + Continuation The Flood: Temptation, Preservation, and the Hells

The Flood: Temptation, Preservation, and the Hells

📋 Summary

The Flood narrative covers two Genesis chapters and is read by Swedenborg as the description of the temptation of Noah — representing the new kind of spiritual man who emerges after the celestial era has ended. The rising waters are not physical destruction but the psychological state of being overwhelmed by falsities and evil persuasions: “the fluctuating” state of a soul undergoing deep inner upheaval. The ark is the interior of the mind, the protected space where genuine spiritual goods are preserved. The various creatures entering by sevens and twos encode specific qualities of the new church’s internal organisation. The forty days and nights represent a complete cycle of temptation. The recession of waters marks the gradual emergence of truth and good after the temptation subsides. Between chapters 7 and 8, Swedenborg inserts his most sustained account of the Hells — first-person descriptions of specific infernal societies encountered during his spirit journeys. These are not allegorical: he reports visiting the cadaverous hell of those who cherished murderous hatred (describing the stench that forced nearby spirits to flee and made him vomit), the hell of cruel manipulators who sent a child-like entity with a concealed dagger and a poisoned cup, and the deepest regions where those who desired to destroy souls are tormented by vivid serpents.

“Such spirits as cherish deadly hatred, and hence breathe out vengeance and nothing less than death to another, knowing no rest till then, are kept in the deepest cadaverous hell, where there is a noisome stench as of carcasses… Certain genii, or rather furies, who were sent forth thence in order that I might know their quality, infected the sphere with such poisonous and pestilent breath that the spirits about me could not stay; and at the same time it so affected my stomach that I vomited.”
— Arcana Coelestia §814
“They manifested themselves under the appearance of a little child, of not uncomely face, with a concealed dagger, whom they sent to me, bearing a cup in his hand. From this it was given me to know that they had a mind to murder, either with the dagger or with poison, under a show of innocence.”
— Arcana Coelestia §814
◆ The phenomenology of the Hells

Swedenborg’s hell accounts are uniquely specific. He identifies particular infernal societies by the quality of their dominant love — hatred, cruelty, avarice, adultery, deceit — and by the spatial region they inhabit (to the left, in the plane of the left temple, descending into specific depths). The torments of hell are not externally imposed by God but are the natural experience of a soul whose love is fundamentally inverted — a soul that delights in hatred experiences its delight as torment when that hatred has nothing external to discharge onto. The stench of the cadaverous hell is real, not figurative: it is the spiritual correspondence of the love of destroying others. Hell, for Swedenborg, is not punishment — it is the natural environment of those who love it.

Genesis 9 The Rainbow Covenant, Noah’s Drunkenness, and the Three Post-Flood Churches

The Rainbow Covenant, Noah’s Drunkenness, and the Three Post-Flood Churches

📋 Summary

Genesis 9 covers two major symbolic moments: the covenant of the rainbow and the incident of Noah’s drunkenness. The rainbow covenant is not a promise about floods but a description of the relationship between the Lord and the newly formed post-Flood spiritual humanity — the “bow in the cloud” representing the state of the regenerate person in whom natural truth (cloud/rain) is illuminated by celestial love (light), producing a visible correspondence. Noah’s drunkenness is a turning point: Noah “began to be a man of the ground” — he became more external, more earthly, than the celestial man he had been. The vine is the new church’s spiritual truth; the wine is the good of that truth; drunkenness is the state of being immersed in charity so completely that wisdom becomes obscured. Ham’s seeing of his father’s nakedness represents a son church that exposed the parent church’s limitations without covering them charitably. Shem and Japheth, walking backward to cover Noah, represent son churches that preserved what was salvageable with reverence. The three sons — Shem, Ham, Japheth — represent the three great religious traditions that descend from the post-Flood church: each inheriting a different aspect of the original knowledge, some preserving more, some less.

“The covenant here treated of is not only concerning the waters of the flood, but also, in the internal sense, concerning the state of the regenerate man after temptation… The ‘bow in the cloud’ signifies the state of the spiritual man in whom the things of the understanding are illuminated by the Lord’s mercy and love.”
— Arcana Coelestia (on Genesis 9:12–13, paraphrase)
“‘Noah began to be a man of the ground’: by which is described that the new church, when no longer internal but external, began to cultivate the lower or natural understanding… for to be ‘a man of the ground’ means to be of the natural rather than the spiritual man.”
— Arcana Coelestia (on Genesis 9:20, paraphrase)
◆ The complete arc: from celestial to spiritual to natural

Genesis 1–9 traces a complete arc of spiritual decline: the Most Ancient Church (Genesis 1–5) was celestial — the highest possible spiritual state, perceiving truth directly from love without needing faith as intermediary. Its deterioration (Genesis 6) into the Nephilim state required the Flood as a reset. The post-Flood church (Noah and his sons) is spiritual — a step lower, operating through faith rather than direct perception. This is already a diminishment. Swedenborg’s entire historical theology can be read as a description of progressive spiritual decline from the Most Ancient Church’s direct perception, through faith-based spirituality, toward the purely natural and materialistic consciousness that characterises modern humanity. The Arcana Coelestia itself is offered as a means of partial restoration: a systematic decoding of the internal sense that was once directly perceived, now requiring learned interpretation because direct perception has been lost.

Reception What Kant Read, What He Suppressed, and Why It Matters

What Kant Read, What He Suppressed, and Why It Matters

📋 Summary

Swedenborg published Arcana Coelestia in eight volumes between 1749 and 1756. It was not widely read — the work was expensive, in Latin, and made extraordinary claims without historical drama or literary flourish. In 1763 a series of pamphlets appeared mocking a Swedish seer who claimed to communicate with the dead, which attracted the attention of Immanuel Kant. Kant acquired all eight volumes and read them. In 1766 he published Dreams of a Spirit-Seer Elucidated by Dreams of Metaphysics — a witty, apparently dismissive text that nevertheless engages Swedenborg’s system with surprising seriousness. Kant’s official position was scepticism, but his actual engagement was more ambivalent. His earliest writings show him grappling with the real possibility that Swedenborg’s accounts of spirit-world topology — the specific spatial location of different spirit societies, the direction of their movements — might be empirically checkable against other witnesses’ accounts. The problem Swedenborg posed for Kant was not lunacy but consistency: the accounts were too internally coherent, and in some cases too convergent with independent reports, to be obviously dismissed. Kant’s eventual solution — to construct a philosophical framework in which such matters are placed permanently beyond the reach of rational investigation, in the unknowable noumenal realm — can be read as a sophisticated response to the Swedenborgian challenge: not a refutation but a quarantine.

“I cannot reconcile myself to the view that it is impossible that there should be something real in what has been reported of Swedenborg’s visionary communications… The impudence with which such things are related, and the credibility of many, demand somewhat more careful investigation before these stories are contemptuously rejected.”
— Kant, private letter to Charlotte von Knobloch, 1763
◆ The Jorjani thesis

Jason Jorjani’s reading — detailed in Prometheus and Atlas and discussed in the Liminal Atlas close reading of his American Alchemy episode — goes further: that Kant’s early writings explicitly acknowledge that the Swedenborgian evidence was too socially destabilising to be released without a philosophical containment strategy, and that the Critical philosophy was designed partly for this purpose. This reading is contested but not absurd — Kant’s Critiques do systematically dismantle the possibility of genuine knowledge claims about any non-empirical reality, including the spirit world Swedenborg claimed to have mapped.

◊ Swedenborg’s influence on the 19th century

Despite the work’s obscurity, Swedenborg’s impact on 19th-century thought was substantial. William Blake engaged him directly and critically, incorporating his cosmology into his own mythological system while rejecting his conservative social ethics. Ralph Waldo Emerson dedicated a chapter of Representative Men to Swedenborg as “the Mystic.” Balzac’s Séraphîta presents a Swedenborgian angel. The New Jerusalem Church (Swedenborgians) was founded in 1787 and persists. More recently, Swedenborg’s accounts of the afterlife have been compared to modern near-death experience research — particularly his description of the resuscitation process, the presence of angelic beings at the moment of death, and the life-review — with researchers noting their structural convergence with NDEs that had not yet been systematically studied in Swedenborg’s era.

GLOSSARY8 key terms

Key Terms

Internal Sense
Swedenborg’s term for the hidden spiritual meaning encoded in every word of scripture. Distinct from the “literal sense” visible to ordinary reading. The internal sense operates through a system of correspondences in which every natural object (light, water, animal, plant) maps precisely to a spiritual state. Swedenborg claims this system was revealed to him directly by angels and confirmed by personal observation in the spiritual world.
Correspondence
The structural relationship between the natural world and the spiritual world: every natural phenomenon is an effect of a spiritual cause, and therefore encodes that cause as its meaning. The natural world is a continuous symbolic expression of the spiritual world. Swedenborg claims this is not metaphor but ontological fact: light does not merely represent understanding — understanding produces its natural correspondent, light, as its expression.
Celestial Man
The highest level of human spiritual development, characterised by perception of divine truth directly from love, without need of faith as intermediary. The Most Ancient Church (pre-Flood humanity) was of this type. Distinguished from the spiritual man (who operates through faith) and the natural/dead man (who operates through sensory evidence alone).
Remains
The preserved deposits of innocence, love, and genuine goodness stored without a person’s knowledge in their interior by the Lord from childhood onward. Essential to any possibility of regeneration: without remains, a soul has no internal ground from which spiritual development can begin. The Nephilim had essentially no remains left — hence the Flood as the only possible reset.
Perception
Direct spiritual sensation of truth and good, without the mediation of doctrinal faith or sensory evidence. The faculty of the celestial man. Distinct from reason (which arrives at truth by inference) and from faith (which believes what is not yet perceived). The loss of perception is, in Swedenborg’s account, the central meaning of the Fall: humanity shifted from perceiving divine truth directly to needing to believe it on authority.
Grand Man (Maximus Homo)
Swedenborg’s term for heaven as a whole, understood as a single cosmic human being. The societies of heaven correspond to organs and regions of the human body: societies organised around love near the heart, those organised around wisdom near the brain and lungs, those providing overview near the eyes. Each individual angel and each society is “an image of the universal heaven” — a fractal repetition of the whole.
The Most Ancient Church
Swedenborg’s term for pre-Flood humanity — not a historical institution but a spiritual type. They had direct celestial perception, communicating with angels through influx rather than language, and receiving truth through love rather than faith. The ten patriarchs of Genesis 5 name successive “church states” or epochs of this Most Ancient Church as it developed and then deteriorated.
Proprium
Latin for “one’s own.” Swedenborg’s term for the self-derived will — the love of one’s own ideas, impulses, and power, as distinct from love that flows from the Lord through the soul. The proprium is identified with Eve in Genesis 2–3 and is the faculty that the serpent (sensory reasoning) persuades toward self-trust rather than divine perception. The regeneration process involves the gradual subordination of the proprium to divine love.
