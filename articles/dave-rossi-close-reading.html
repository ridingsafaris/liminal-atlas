Physics Explainer: Maxwell → Heaviside → Extended Electrodynamics

Maxwell (1865): Published electromagnetic theory as 20 equations in 20 unknowns, using quaternion mathematics. Scalar electric potential (φ) and magnetic vector potential (A) were first-class physical objects.

Heaviside & Gibbs (1880s): Reformulated into 4 modern vector equations. Applied the Lorenz gauge condition (∂φ/∂t + ∇·A = 0), which constrains the potentials in a way that eliminates longitudinal wave solutions from free space. This simplification is valid for all known laboratory phenomena.

Extended electrodynamics (MCE): Removes or relaxes the gauge constraint. This generates additional "scalar longitudinal wave" (SLW) solutions. Proponents (Zohuri, Barrett, Bearden) argue these modes are physically real. Critics say they are mathematical degrees of freedom with no observable consequence — and that QED is the most precisely tested theory in history, leaving no room for such effects.

The Aharonov-Bohm effect (Yakir Aharonov & David Bohm, 1959; confirmed experimentally by Tonomura et al., 1986): An electron passing through a field-free region near a solenoid is phase-shifted by the magnetic vector potential A — even though B=0 in the region the electron travels. This proves potentials are physically real, not just mathematical conveniences. Rossi uses this as a foundation for taking scalar potential physics seriously.

Current Scientific Status of Scalar Waves

Longitudinal waves are real and undisputed in acoustics (sound waves in air), seismology (P-waves), and plasma physics (Langmuir / plasma waves, Birkeland currents). These are well-understood phenomena. The controversy is specifically about longitudinal electromagnetic waves propagating in free space — a solution allowed by unconstrained Maxwell equations but excluded in standard theory via the gauge condition.

As of 2025, no peer-reviewed, independently replicated experiment has demonstrated free-space EM longitudinal wave propagation. Theoretical frameworks exist — papers by Bahman Zohuri, Konstantin Meyl, and others appear in journals. But mainstream quantum electrodynamics (QED) — tested to 1 part in 10¹² — has no room for these effects, and all apparent demonstrations have been explained by conventional EM or experimental artefact.

Nikola Tesla1856–1943↗ Wikipedia↗ Wardenclyffe Tower

Inventor of AC power systems, the induction motor, and foundational radio technology. In Colorado Springs experiments (1899) and the Wardenclyffe Tower project, Tesla claimed to have transmitted electrical power longitudinally through the earth as a "resonant ground wave" — not conventional EM radiation. He believed this demonstrated a mode of energy propagation missed by Hertzian wave theory. This is the historical anchor for all modern scalar wave claims.

Physics Explainer: EM Fields, Space-Time Curvature, and GPS Time Dilation

Can EM fields curve space-time? Yes — in General Relativity. Einstein's field equations (1915) require that ALL forms of energy curve space-time, including electromagnetic field energy (via the stress-energy tensor). This is exact. But the practical curvature from any laboratory EM source is ~10⁴⁰ times too small to measure. Rossi argues that spin, rotation, and superconducting resonance create nonlinear amplification not captured in linearised approximations.

Biefeld-Brown Effect: Thomas Townsend Brown (1920s–1950s) observed apparent thrust from high-voltage asymmetric capacitors. The U.S. Navy's Project Winterhaven (1952) investigated the effect. Most physicists attribute the effect to ionic airflow (ion wind) — but Brown conducted tests claiming thrust in vacuum. The debate is not fully settled in the literature.

Thomas Townsend Brown1905–1985↗ Wikipedia↗ Biefeld-Brown Effect

Project Winterhaven (1952) proposed a manned disc aircraft based on his principles. He spent his career convinced he had discovered an EM-gravity link. The mechanism remains disputed.

Physics Explainer: Superconductivity, BCS Theory & Gravitomagnetism

Superconductivity: Discovered by Heike Kamerlingh Onnes (1911). Below a critical temperature Tc, certain materials conduct with zero resistance and expel magnetic fields (Meissner effect). BCS Theory (Bardeen, Cooper, Schrieffer, 1957; Nobel 1972): Cooper pairs of electrons form via lattice phonon interaction and condense into a single quantum state — a Bose-Einstein condensate of fermion pairs.

Gravitomagnetism / Frame-dragging: A real prediction of General Relativity. A rotating mass drags space-time around it (Lense-Thirring effect). Confirmed by NASA's Gravity Probe B (2011) for Earth's rotation. The effect is ~10⁻¹⁴ rad/s for Earth — unmeasurably small for laboratory masses.

Ning Li's proposal: coherently rotating ions in a superconducting lattice could produce a collective gravitomagnetic moment amplified by quantum coherence — potentially orders of magnitude beyond Lense-Thirring predictions. A 2010 DIA analysis found the predicted effects would be ~10²⁰ times smaller than Li and Torr claimed.

Biology Explainer: DNA Structure & Triplex DNA

Standard DNA: A double-stranded helix (Watson, Crick, Franklin, Wilkins — 1953). Two anti-parallel strands held by complementary base pairing (A-T, C-G). The base sequence encodes all genetic information. This is among the most robustly confirmed structures in all of science.

Triplex DNA is a real, documented laboratory phenomenon (not speculative). A third strand of DNA or RNA can bind to the major groove of the double helix via Hoogsteen base pairing. Triplex structures are studied for gene silencing and potential cancer therapy. This is entirely different from the "electromagnetic third strand" Rossi describes, but the terminology overlap is worth noting.

An electromagnetic informational component of DNA — beyond the chemical molecule — appears in Montagnier's work, in Russian biophysicist Garjajev's "DNA as a biocomputer" claims (largely unverified), and in various contact experiencer narratives. It is not accepted in mainstream molecular biology.

Physics Explainer: Time Dilation & GPS — This Part Is Correct

Time dilation is experimentally confirmed by both Special Relativity (moving clocks run slow) and General Relativity (clocks in stronger gravity run slow). GPS satellites orbit at ~20,200 km altitude in a weaker gravitational field. Their clocks run ~45 μs/day fast (GR) and ~7 μs/day slow (SR orbit velocity) — net: ~38 μs/day fast. If uncorrected, GPS position errors would accumulate at ~10 km/day. The correction runs continuously. Rossi's use of this as evidence that time can be affected by gravity manipulation is factually grounded. His extrapolation to "time weapons" is speculative.

What to Make of All This

Dave Rossi is not a credentialled physicist. His specific claims — scalar longitudinal waves, superconductor gravity effects, DNA teleportation, electromagnetic third strands — sit in contested, unverified, or outright rejected regions of the scientific map. Extended electrodynamics has peer-reviewed papers but no independently replicated experiments.

And yet every major figure he cites has made serious claims at the frontier of accepted knowledge. Ning Li's papers are in Physical Review. Montagnier won the Nobel Prize. Pais's patents were defended by the U.S. Navy. Penrose won the Nobel. The history of physics is a history of contested edges — not a smooth consensus.

What Rossi represents is the liminal technologist: formed by anomalous experience, drawn to heterodox physics, apparently taken seriously by people with clearances and institutional access. That defence officials reportedly listened to a construction worker's presentations on superconductors says something — even if we're not yet sure what it says.

This blog exists to sit at exactly these intersections — not credulously, not dismissively, but with the discipline of checking primary sources and the honesty to say what is verified and what is not.
