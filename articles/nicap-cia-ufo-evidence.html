CIA vs. NICAP
The UFO Evidence & the CIA’s Monitoring of Civilian Oversight
1956–1969

Document: CIA-RDP81R00560R000100010001-0 Classification: UNCLASSIFIED (civilian publication filed by CIA) Declassified: 2001/04/02 3 primary documents · 20-event timeline

CIA document CIA-RDP81R00560R000100010001-0 is not a CIA-authored assessment. It is NICAP’s own 1964 congressional brief, The UFO Evidence, edited by Richard H. Hall — 388 pages, 746 cases, 14 sections — which the CIA obtained and filed in its CREST intelligence database, where it remained on four physical terminals in Washington DC until a 2017 MuckRock lawsuit forced it online. Its presence in CIA files is the document’s story: the agency was treating a civilian research organisation’s output as intelligence material requiring active monitoring. Read alongside the CIA’s internal NICAP profile (document 0005516120) and its 1997 institutional history, a detailed picture emerges of how the CIA managed, penetrated, and ultimately neutralised the most credible civilian UAP oversight body of the Cold War era.

The Central Irony

NICAP’s Board of Governors included Roscoe H. Hillenkoetter — the founding Director of the CIA — publicly calling for Congress to investigate the CIA’s suppression of UFO evidence. The CIA’s response was to debate internally whether to show their former director the classified documents he was demanding be released, as a way to neutralise him. He resigned from the board in 1962 under circumstances the CIA’s own history declines to fully explain. NICAP was eventually run into the ground by a successor director whose CIA connections were later confirmed in interviews. Cross-reference: Australian JIO File 3092/2 names both Fahrney and Hillenkoetter as officers who publicly broke from official policy.

Document context

This close reading covers three related documents: NICAP’s The UFO Evidence (1964) as filed by CIA; an internal CIA profile of NICAP’s operations (~1966); and the CIA’s 1997 declassified institutional history of its UFO involvement. Together they document the complete arc of the CIA’s relationship with the most credible civilian UAP oversight body of the Cold War era.

📄 The UFO Evidence (1964) — NICAP’s 388-page congressional brief, filed by CIA
CIA-RDP81R00560R000100010001-0 UNCLASSIFIED (NICAP publication) Civilian Report — obtained and filed by CIA 1964 (CIA filing date: declassified 2001/04/02)
Origin: National Investigations Committee on Aerial Phenomena (NICAP), 1536 Connecticut Ave. NW, Washington DC  →  Filed by: CIA CREST database — Central Intelligence Agency
The primary document — CIA-RDP81R00560R000100010001-0 — is not a CIA-authored assessment of NICAP. It is NICAP’s own flagship publication, “The UFO Evidence” (1964), edited by Richard H. Hall. The CIA obtained a copy and filed it in their CREST intelligence database, where it remained accessible only on four physical terminals in Washington DC until a 2017 MuckRock lawsuit forced it online. The document’s presence in the CIA’s own filing system is itself significant: the agency was actively monitoring NICAP’s published output and treating a civilian research report as intelligence material. The report is 388 pages, presenting 746 selected cases from NICAP’s database of over 5,000 signed reports, organised into fourteen sections targeting a congressional audience.
“The foremost question which remains is: What are the UFOs? The importance of these objects, if the above hypothesis is correct, is readily apparent. In order to settle this question, we strongly recommend that a much larger scale and more thorough scientific investigation be undertaken.”

📄 CIA internal profile of NICAP — budget, membership, ‘Project AcDas’ computer programme
CIA-0005516120 SECRET Internal CIA Intelligence Assessment c. 1966–1967 (declassified)
Origin: CIA Contact Division / Office of Scientific Intelligence  →  Filed by: CIA internal files
A separate CIA document (0005516120) provides a live intelligence profile of NICAP’s operations, almost certainly written during the 1966–67 UFO flap when NICAP’s membership peaked at ~14,000. It reveals the CIA was tracking NICAP’s internal structure in granular detail: budget ($640,000/year), membership (3,500 dues-paying at time of writing, expected to rise substantially), and a classified computer programme inside NICAP codenamed “Project AcDas” — an effort to develop pattern analysis from NICAP’s 15,000+ raw reports. The CIA profiled AcDas’s data schema: events (time, place, terrain, weather, speed, colour, shape), principals (witness data, medical info, psychological aspects), and investigation notes. The document also profiles NICAP’s investigator network: at the time of writing, 35 investigators nationwide, with 7 PhDs, 2 MAs/MSs, 23 BAs/BSs, and 24 with college training. 12 of 35 were pilots. The CIA was building an intelligence picture of a civilian oversight body as if it were a foreign target.

📄 CIA’s official history of its UFO involvement — written after DCI Woolsey’s 1993 review
Studies in Intelligence Vol.1 No.1 1997 UNCLASSIFIED (declassified) CIA Institutional History 1997 — Gerald K. Haines, CIA historian
Origin: CIA Office of the Historian  →  Filed by: Studies in Intelligence — CIA’s internal journal, declassified
The most comprehensive CIA self-account of its UFO activities. Written by CIA historian Gerald K. Haines after DCI R. James Woolsey ordered a full review of CIA UFO files in 1993. Key revelations relevant to the NICAP story: (1) CIA officials debated showing Hillenkoetter the Robertson Panel report “as a possible way to defuse the situation” — they were worried he would use his NICAP platform to force disclosure; (2) CIA officer Frank Chapin hinted that UFO researcher Leon Davidson “might have ulterior motives, some of them perhaps not in the best interest of this country” and suggested FBI investigation; (3) In 1964, DCI John McCone — prompted by White House discussions on what to do if alien intelligence were discovered in space — asked for an updated CIA evaluation of UFOs; OSI officers then met directly with Richard H. Hall, NICAP’s acting director, to obtain NICAP’s recent reports and samples; (4) Hillenkoetter resigned from NICAP’s board in 1962 — the CIA history does not record whether CIA pressure was applied, but notes the agency had been actively concerned about Hillenkoetter’s insider knowledge.
“CIA officials confirmed their opposition to the declassification of the full report and worried that Keyhoe had the ear of former DCI VAdm. Roscoe Hillenkoetter, who served on the board of governors of NICAP.”

Founded by physicist T. Townsend Brown alongside retired officers. Initial mission: scientific investigation of UFOs and forcing full public disclosure by responsible authorities. Already on the CIA's radar before it formally began operations.

NICAP’s first major public statement. Board Chairman Rear Admiral Delmer S. Sets NICAP’s technical, credibility-based tone for the next decade.

NICAP Maj. Donald E. Keyhoe elected Director; Fahrney as Board Chair
Keyhoe — retired Marine Corps Major, Naval Academy classmate of Hillenkoetter, and already the author of three bestselling UFO books — assumes the directorship. His strategy: use elite military credibility to force congressional hearings. He immediately recruits Hillenkoetter to the board.

CIA CIA begins monitoring NICAP; Robertson Panel report under pressure
NICAP and researcher Edward Ruppelt (former Blue Book head) publicly reveal the existence of the Robertson Panel. NICAP immediately demands full release. CIA’s Philip Strong (Deputy Asst. The first Director of the CIA — Hillenkoetter had run the agency from its founding in 1947 until 1950 — joins the board of the organisation dedicated to exposing the government’s UFO cover-up. He publicly accuses the Air Force of misleading the American public and urges Congress to act to ‘reduce the danger from secrecy.’ The CIA’s concern is immediate.

CIA’s Contact Division approaches Ralph C. Mayher (KYW-TV photographer) for copies of 1952 UFO photographs. After analysis, they are returned without comment. When Mayher asks for CIA’s evaluation, the CIA officer John Hazen declines to respond. Keyhoe publicly charges the CIA is deliberately asking eyewitnesses not to make sightings public.

From the NICAP board, the former CIA Director publicly calls for Congress to investigate UFO secrecy. CIA officials are alarmed. They debate whether to show Hillenkoetter the full classified Robertson Panel report — the document CIA had actively suppressed — as a way to neutralise his advocacy.

Rear Adm. Delmer S. Fahrney
NICAP Board Chair · Father of Navy Guided Missiles
Retired Rear Admiral who directed the US Navy’s guided missile programme from its inception. NICAP’s first Board Chairman. In January 1957 he called the first NICAP press conference declaring UFOs were entering the atmosphere at high speeds performing accelerations beyond known capabilities — directed by some form of intelligence. He resigned in April 1957, citing his wife’s illness, though he was also troubled by the ridicule his UAP interests attracted among his military peers. VADM Roscoe H. Hillenkoetter
First CIA Director 1947–1950 · NICAP Board Governor 1957–1962
The founding Director of the CIA, serving from the agency’s creation in 1947 until 1950. Naval Academy classmate of Major Keyhoe. Joined NICAP’s board in 1957 and publicly accused the Air Force of misleading Americans and urged Congress to act to ‘reduce the danger from secrecy.’ The CIA actively worried about his insider knowledge — debating whether to brief him on the Robertson Panel to neutralise him. He resigned from NICAP’s board in 1962 under pressure from the CIA following Air Force complaints, abruptly reversing his public position. His behaviour in the years between — sending contradictory signals to Keyhoe and to sceptic Donald Menzel — remains one of the murkiest figures in UAP disclosure history.

Maj. Donald E. Keyhoe

Retired Marine Corps Major and author of three bestselling UFO books in 1950, 1953, and 1955, in which he argued the Air Force knew UFOs were extraterrestrial and was covering it up. Cross-reference · Australian JIO File 3092/2 (1957–1971)

The Australian intelligence community’s 14-year SECRET file on UFOs names Admiral Fahrney and Admiral Hillenkoetter as retired officers who publicly stated the US government knew UFOs were extraterrestrial. The JIO file and the NICAP documents are two parallel threads of the same story: governments treating UAP as a real intelligence problem while publicly denying it, and civilian oversight bodies populated by insiders who knew enough to make the cover-up difficult to maintain.
