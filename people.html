Figure Thomas Townsend Brown
Inventor · Biefeld-Brown Effect · 1905–1985
Ohio-born electrical engineer and inventor who in the early 1920s discovered what he called electrogravitic thrust: movement produced by a high-voltage asymmetric capacitor. Discovered the effect using Coolidge X-ray tubes while at Caltech under Robert Millikan. Studied under Paul Biefield at Denison University. Demonstrated vacuum thrust in Paris (1955) at the Montgolfier facilities and at Bahnson Labs in North Carolina (1958). Funded in the 1960s by Floyd Odlum (Northrop majority owner) at Guidance Technologies, Santa Monica. Associated with Bill Lear, Edward Teller, and Robert Sarbacher. His 1954 Winterhaven proposal named magnesium-bismuth as the ideal high-K dielectric — the same material appearing in alleged UAP crash samples analysed by Gary Nolan.

Figure Miguel Alcubierre
Theoretical Physicist · UNAM · Warp Drive Theorist
Mexican theoretical physicist at the National Autonomous University of Mexico (UNAM). In 1994 proposed the Alcubierre metric — a solution to Einstein’s field equations that permits faster-than-light travel by contracting spacetime ahead of a vessel and expanding it behind, without the vessel itself exceeding c locally. Often described as the “warp drive” paper. Requires negative energy density, whose physical existence remains unconfirmed. NASA has funded research into whether a practical implementation is conceivable. Discussed by Eric Hazelton as one of the “not impossible” propulsion mechanisms.

Figure Rolf Landauer
IBM Fellow · Information Physics Pioneer · 1927–1999
German-American physicist and IBM Fellow who proposed Landauer’s principle (1961): erasing one bit of information must release a minimum amount of heat into the environment, establishing a fundamental thermodynamic cost of computation.
