Overview

Vice Admiral Thomas R. Wilson served as Director of the Defense Intelligence Agency (DIA) and as Deputy Director of Intelligence (J2) on the Joint Chiefs of Staff. He had oversight of US military intelligence operations, including theoretically the entire budget of classified programmes run through DIA funding channels.

Wilson is the central figure in the Wilson-Davis memo — a 15-page handwritten document by Eric Davis recording a meeting they had in the summer of 1997 in the parking lot of EG&G Special Projects near Las Vegas’s McCarron Airport. According to the notes, Wilson had been called back from retirement to active duty to close a classified project at “the unacknowledged facility near the Nevada test site” (Area 51). Davis was introduced to Wilson through two National Nuclear Security Agency officials who were members of the Association of Former Intelligence Officers, forming a Las Vegas chapter.

In the meeting, Wilson told Davis he had discovered that his own DIA director’s budget was funding a classified crash retrieval programme through innocuous budget codes he had never been read into. When he confronted the corporate programme manager, security chief, legal counsel, and chief scientist of the aerospace company holding the programme, he was told: “This is a crash retrieval. Non-human intelligence, non-human technology, off-world. But you don’t have need-to-know.” Wilson was reportedly frustrated and stunned.

The notes were given as a courtesy copy to Apollo astronaut Edgar Mitchell, who was supposed to destroy them after one year. Mitchell died in 2016 without leaving instructions; his estate released them publicly in 2019. In this episode, Davis confirms on camera that the notes are “100% real, 100% accurate” but cannot confirm the meeting itself occurred for legal and security reasons.
