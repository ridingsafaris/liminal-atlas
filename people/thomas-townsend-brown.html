Biography

Ohio-born physicist and inventor (1905–1985) who discovered electrogravitic thrust in the early 1920s at Caltech under Robert Millikan. His Biefeld-Brown Effect — that a high-voltage asymmetric capacitor produces directional thrust in a vacuum, pointing toward a link between electromagnetism and gravity — is the central claim of his life's work. He studied the effect in collaboration with Paul Alfred Biefeld (who studied alongside Einstein), naming it after him.

His Navy service records were classified; his 1929 paper The Minor Quantum disappeared entirely; his 1953 Winterhaven Proposal blueprinting a flying saucer programme almost exactly matches insider accounts of America's black aerospace programme. The Navy denied his records existed, gave his daughter another man's service file, and ultimately admitted the records might be classified beyond acknowledgement.

Brown pursued an intentional self-discrediting strategy — the 'Wounded Prairie Chicken Routine' (Paul Shatkin's term) — showing observers the wrong version of his work to protect the real solid-dielectric gravitator from Soviet intelligence, after a spy was discovered in a 1950 Pearl Harbor demonstration.

His connections span the entire Cold War intelligence landscape: he parachuted into Nazi Germany in 1945 alongside William Stephenson (MI6's 'Intrepid') and Wild Bill Donovan (OSS) to retrieve exotic propulsion technology; participated in the Caroline Group private intelligence network; worked with Robert Sarbacher (Harvard physicist, Einstein student, covert National Research Labs director, later confirmed UAP crash retrieval); was witnessed by Edward Teller (who said 'I have no idea what makes this work'); co-founded NICAP as a probable counter-intelligence honey trap; and picked up UFO crash material in Hartford, Connecticut, front-running Project Blue Book with credentials that remain unexplained.

His Guidance Technologies company (Santa Monica) shared a block with Bill Lear's corporation — the two inventors had lunch together daily. When Brown abruptly shut it down in 1967, Northrop published and then retracted a press release about investing in electroaerodynamics. The B2 stealth bomber, built by the merged Northrop-Grumman whose major investor Floyd Odlum was the same man who invested in Brown's company, may implement the Biefeld-Brown principle as an electrostatic lift-enhancing effect on its wings.

He spent his final years on Catalina Island running electrometers measuring sidereal radiation — anomalies in his gravitator's performance that correlated with celestial positions, suggesting the gravitational constant may not be constant. His daughter Linda recalls a $100,000 cheque from the Townsend Brown Foundation to Stanford Research Institute — where the CIA's Stargate remote viewing programme was housed. A NASA mission controller named Timothy Taylor (Diana Pasulka's 'Tyler') told a personal friend he was in an elite group called The Nassau Group, headed by Townsend Brown, with time travel technology.
