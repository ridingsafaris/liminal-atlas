Biography

John Edward Mack (1929–2004) was a Pulitzer Prize-winning professor of psychiatry at Harvard Medical School, best known in the latter part of his career for his serious academic investigation of the alien abduction phenomenon. He won the Pulitzer Prize in 1977 for his biography of T.E. Lawrence, A Prince of Our Disorder.

Beginning in the late 1980s, Mack became deeply engaged with accounts of alien abduction experiences. Rather than dismissing them as delusion or psychopathology, he documented them with clinical rigor in Abduction: Human Encounters with Aliens (1994) and Passport to the Cosmos (1999). He founded PEER (Program for Extraordinary Experience Research) at Harvard to study these encounters systematically.

Harvard conducted a formal faculty inquiry into whether Mack had violated academic standards by taking abduction accounts seriously. The inquiry ultimately upheld his academic freedom while noting concerns about his methodology. Mack was a friend and colleague of Thomas Kuhn (The Structure of Scientific Revolutions), who encouraged his unconventional work.

Mack was killed in London in September 2004 when struck by a drunk driver after attending a lecture. He remains the most credentialed academic to have conducted serious empirical research into the abduction phenomenon.
